\begin{abstract}

Food production is the process by which land, water, labor, and other inputs are coordinated to generate the nutrients that sustain human populations -- and optimizing it at scale is one of the defining resource allocation challenges of the coming decades, as rising demand, climate volatility, and sustainability constraints simultaneously tighten. We study a multi-objective crop allocation problem balancing nutritional value, environmental sustainability, and affordability for 27 distinct crops. We thereby evaluate the engineering and mathematical trade-offs of mapping large-scale structured tasks to quantum annealing hardware. The problem is modeled as a Binary Plot (BP) formulation: a grid of fixed-area plots, each assigned to exactly one crop, expressed as a Constrained Quadratic Model (CQM). We benchmark our approach against the classical Gurobi solver using problems with 10 to 1{,}000 land patches and run two experiments: (1) a comparative analysis utilizing D-Wave's hybrid solvers, and (2) an evaluation of pure Quantum Processing Unit (QPU) execution using custom decomposition algorithms. Our results demonstrate that migrating the discrete BP formulation to quantum hardware exposes severe classical embedding bottlenecks: although QPU execution time scales linearly, the classical preprocessing overhead required to embed densely connected penalty models constitutes 76--86\% of the total runtime. We conclude that for structured allocation tasks, overcoming this classical embedding bottleneck---rather than accelerating QPU computation---remains the primary frontier for realizing quantum utility.
As an exploratory extension, we introduce Problem B (multi-period rotation with frustrated constraints) -- a theoretical investigation not validated with domain experts. Our hierarchical decomposition runs successfully, whereas Gurobi hits 300-second timeouts, though our solutions show constraint violations. Results indicate computational performance is formulation-dependent: classical solvers excel on well-structured linear problems, while quantum annealing shows potential for frustrated quadratic problems.
\end{abstract}

\subsection{Introduction}

Phase 3 of this project represents a comprehensive investigation into the practical application of quantum annealing for large-scale agricultural optimization. Building on the theoretical foundations established in Phase 2, we systematically evaluate D-Wave quantum processing units (QPUs) through three complementary research approaches, each using different problem formulations and solution methodologies.

\subsubsection{Experimental Framework}

Our investigation employs two distinct problem variants evaluated across three studies:

\paragraph{Variant A: Binary Crop Allocation}
A constrained quadratic model with 27 individual crops, linear benefit objective, and diversity constraints. This represents the baseline agricultural optimization problem where we compare quantum and classical approaches.

\paragraph{Variant B: Multi-Period Rotation}
An enhanced formulation with 6 aggregated crop families, 3-period temporal horizon, quadratic rotation synergies, and frustrated spatial interactions. This formulation is deliberately designed to challenge classical solvers while remaining QPU-tractable.

\paragraph{Three Complementary Studies:}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Study 1.A: Hybrid Solver Benchmarking.} We benchmark D-Wave's Leap hybrid solvers against classical Gurobi optimization using Variant A across multiple scales. This establishes the baseline quantum performance of the most popular solver offered by DWave \cite{dwave_hybrid_solver}.
    
    \item \textbf{Study 2.A: QPU Decomposition.} We develop and evaluate seven decomposition strategies (HybridGrid, PlotBased, Multilevel, Louvain, Spectral, Coordinated, CQM-First, PlotBased) on Variant A to address the issue with embedding large problems (more detail in \cref{appx:decomp}). This provides tunability of the solver parameters, contrary to the Hybrid Solvers.
    
    \item \textbf{Study 2.B: Quantum \textit{Improvement} Demonstration.} Using Variant B, we test how a specific hybrid quantum-classical algorithm can yield better solution quality than Gurobi. This formulation is included to investigate problem characteristics where quantum approaches might offer computational improvements too.
    
\end{enumerate}


\subsection{Problem Formulations}

\subsubsection{Common Definitions}



Let's first define the common sets and indices:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\mathcal{F}$: Set of farms with land availability $L_p$ for $f \in \mathcal{F}$
    \item $\mathcal{G}$: Set of crop families (or groups), indexed by $g$.
    \item $\mathcal{C}$: Set of crops 

\end{itemize}

Each crop $c$ is characterized by a composite benefit score $B_c$ computed from five weighted components:

\begin{equation}
B_c = w_{nv} \cdot v_{nv,c} + w_{nd} \cdot v_{nd,c} - w_{ei} \cdot v_{ei,c} + w_{af} \cdot v_{af,c} + w_{su} \cdot v_{su,c}
\label{eq:benefit}
\end{equation}

Further explaination is contained in \cref{subs:data}





\subsubsection{Problem Variant A: Crop Allocation CQM}
\label{subsec:formulation_a}

\paragraph{Description and Problem Structure}

The crop allocation formulation addresses the fundamental agricultural planning question: 

\begin{quote}
    \textit{Given a collection of land plots, which crop should be assigned to each plot to maximize overall benefit while satisfying dietary diversity and area constraints?}
\end{quote} 


This allocation problem serves as our baseline formulation, enabling direct comparison between quantum and classical approaches on well-structured optimization instances.

The formulation balances multiple competing objectives through a composite benefit score that integrates nutritional value, environmental sustainability, and economic accessibility for each crop \cite{esteso_sustainable_2023}. Constraints encode both physical limitations (land capacity, minimum planting areas) and policy requirements (minimum dietary diversity across food groups, bounded total area per crop type). Unlike Variant B's multi-period extension, this formulation omits temporal dynamics and spatial interactions, yielding a problem structure that classical MILP solvers handle efficiently through standard branch-and-bound techniques.

We present two equivalent formulations, each producing the same real-world outcomes despite fundamental differences in their variable structure and computational behavior:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{MILP Formulation (Farm-level with continuous areas):} The natural formulation using continuous allocation variables $A_{f,c} \in [0, L_p]$ representing hectares allocated and binary decision variables $Y_{f,c} \in \{0,1\}$. This formulation has a tight LP relaxation and is solved efficiently by classical solvers like Gurobi.
    
    \item \textbf{BP Formulation (Binary Plot):} A discretized formulation using pure binary variables $Y_{p,c} \in \{0,1\}$ indicating plot-crop assignment, where plots are characterized by constant area. This formulation is directly compatible with QUBO/quantum annealing but has weaker LP bounds.
\end{enumerate}

The distinction between these formulations is critical: they represent the \emph{same} agricultural planning problem but exhibit dramatically different computational behavior. Classical solvers excel on the MILP formulation due to tight LP relaxations, while quantum annealing requires the BP formulation's pure binary structure.

\paragraph{Formulation 1: MILP (Farm-level with Continuous Areas)}

The MILP formulation represents the natural mathematical model of the crop allocation problem, where each farm $f$ can allocate any fraction of its available land $L_p$ to any crop $c$.

\subparagraph{Decision Variables}

\begin{itemize}
    \item $A_{f,c} \in [0, L_f]$: \textbf{Continuous} variable representing the area (in hectares) of farm $f$ allocated to crop $c$
    \item $Y_{f,c} \in \{0,1\}$: Binary indicator variable, $Y_{f,c} = 1$ if crop $c$ is planted on farm $f$ (i.e., $A_{f,c} > 0$)
    \item $U_c \in \{0,1\}$: Binary indicator variable, $U_c = 1$ if crop $c$ is planted on at least one farm
\end{itemize}

\subparagraph{Objective Function}

Maximize total area-weighted benefit normalized by total available land:

\begin{equation}
\max \quad Z_{\text{MILP}} = \frac{1}{A_{\text{total}}} \sum_{f \in \mathcal{F}} \sum_{c \in \mathcal{C}} B_c \cdot A_{f,c}
\label{eq:MILP_objective}
\end{equation}

where $A_{\text{total}} = \sum_{f \in \mathcal{F}} L_p$ is the total land area and $B_c$ is the composite benefit score for crop $c$.

\subparagraph{Variable Count and Computational Characteristics}

For $|\mathcal{F}|$ farms and $|\mathcal{C}|$ crops:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Continuous variables: $|\mathcal{F}| \times |\mathcal{C}|$ (the $A_{f,c}$ allocations)
    \item Binary variables: $|\mathcal{F}| \times |\mathcal{C}| + |\mathcal{C}|$ (the $Y_{f,c}$ and $U_c$ indicators)
    \item Total variables: $\approx 2 \cdot |\mathcal{F}| \times |\mathcal{C}|$
\end{itemize}

The key advantage of this formulation is that the LP relaxation (obtained by relaxing $Y_{f,c}, U_c \in [0,1]$) provides tight bounds on the optimal solution, which is exploited by Gurobi.

\paragraph{Formulation 2: BP (Binary Plot)}

The BP formulation discretizes the land into fixed-size plots, each assigned to exactly one crop.

\subparagraph{Decision Variables}

\begin{itemize}
    \item $Y_{p,c} \in \{0,1\}$: Binary variable indicating whether crop $c$ is planted on plot $p$
    \item $U_c \in \{0,1\}$: Binary variable indicating whether crop $c$ is selected on at least one plot
\end{itemize}

Note the critical difference: in BP, $Y_{p,c}$ represents a \emph{complete assignment} of plot $p$ to crop $c$, not merely an indicator. Each plot has fixed area $L_p$ (typically equal across plots in a uniform grid), as opposed to planting on a fraction of a farm -- where fractions are not necessarily equal in size.

\subparagraph{Objective Function}

Maximize total area-weighted benefit:

\begin{equation}
\max \quad Z_{\text{BP}} = \frac{1}{A_{\text{total}}} \sum_{p \in \mathcal{P}} \sum_{c \in \mathcal{C}} L_p \cdot B_c \cdot Y_{p,c}
\label{eq:bp_objective}
\end{equation}

where $\mathcal{P}$ is the set of plots and $A_{\text{total}} = \sum_{p \in \mathcal{P}} L_p$.

\subparagraph{Variable Count and Computational Characteristics}

For $|\mathcal{P}|$ plots and $|\mathcal{C}|$ crops:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Binary variables: $|\mathcal{P}| \times |\mathcal{C}| + |\mathcal{C}|$ (the $Y_{p,c}$ and $U_c$ variables)
    \item No continuous variables
\end{itemize}


The LP relaxation of BP is weak: relaxing $Y_{p,c} \in [0,1]$ allows fractional assignments that don't correspond to valid solutions, forcing branch-and-bound to explore many more nodes. Empirically, Gurobi solving BP takes 10--100$\times$ longer than solving the equivalent MILP formulation \cite{wolsey1998integer}.

\paragraph{Constraint Implementation Notes}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{MILP constraints} involve both continuous ($A_{f,c}$) and binary ($Y_{f,c}$, $U_c$) variables, leveraging tight LP relaxations for efficient branch-and-bound.
    \item \textbf{BP constraints} are purely binary, enabling direct QUBO conversion but with weaker LP bounds.
    \item The \textbf{one-hot constraint} (Plot Assignment) is critical for BP: when converted to QUBO penalty form, it introduces $O(|\mathcal{C}|^2)$ quadratic interactions per plot.
    \item \textbf{Food group constraints} are identical in both formulations since they only involve the global $U_c$ indicators.
    \item \textbf{Area bounds} in MILP translate to \textbf{plot count bounds} in BP, with the relationship $a_c^{\min} \approx n_c^{\min} \cdot L_p$ for uniform plot areas $L_p$.

    \item \textbf{Hard constraints} are strictly enforced and cannot be violated, eliminating infeasible solutions from the search space entirely.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Relationship Between Formulations}

The two formulations are mathematically related through discretization. If we partition each farm $f$ into $k_f$ equal-sized plots, the MILP allocation $A_{f,c}$ corresponds to:

\begin{equation}
A_{f,c} \approx \frac{L_p}{k_f} \sum_{p \in \mathcal{P}_f} Y_{p,c}
\end{equation}

where $\mathcal{P}_f$ denotes the plots belonging to farm $f$. As $k_f \to \infty$, the BP formulation converges to the MILP formulation (more detail in \cref{app:refinement}).

\paragraph{QUBO Conversion of BP Formulation}

For quantum annealing, the BP constraints must be converted to penalty terms in the objective function. The one-hot constraint becomes:

\begin{equation}
H_{\text{penalty}} = \lambda \sum_{p \in \mathcal{P}} \left( \sum_{c \in \mathcal{C}} Y_{p,c} - 1 \right)^2
\label{eq:qubo_penalty}
\end{equation}

where $\lambda > 0$ is a penalty weight chosen large enough to enforce constraint satisfaction. Expanding the quadratic:

\begin{equation}
H_{\text{penalty}} = \lambda \sum_{p} \left[ \sum_c Y_{p,c} + 2\sum_{c < c'} Y_{p,c} Y_{p,c'} - 2\sum_c Y_{p,c} + 1 \right]
\end{equation}

This introduces $O(|\mathcal{P}| \times |\mathcal{C}|^2)$ quadratic interactions in the QUBO, significantly increasing problem density compared to the linear MILP formulation.

\subsubsection{Problem Variant B: Hierarchical Multi-Period Rotation}
\label{subsec:formulation_b}

\paragraph{Description and Problem Structure}

\textbf{Nomenclature}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Multi-period rotation: rotation of the crop planted in the plot after a certain amount of time, needed to make the soil recover its nutrients
    \item Rotation synergies: if the aforementioned crop rotation is done in the correct order we can improve our food production
    \item Spatial interactions: planting the same crop in adjacent plots, effectively creating a huge monoculture plot, increases the speed at which pests spread
\end{itemize}

Variant B extends the single-period allocation problem to a multi-period rotation planning model with 6 aggregated crop families, a 3-period temporal horizon, quadratic rotation synergies, and spatial interactions. This formulation is designed to introduce components not considered in Variant A that may challenge classical solvers, particularly the quadratic temporal and spatial interaction terms that create a densely connected optimization landscape.

\textbf{Methodological Note}
It is important to acknowledge that no consensus exists in the literature regarding how rotation effects should be mathematically modeled. Our review revealed substantial heterogeneity in formulation. Given this heterogeneity, our formulation should be viewed as \emph{one plausible instantiation} of rotation planning rather than a definitive model.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Yield multipliers vs. additive penalties:} Some models apply rotation effects as multiplicative factors on the decision variables (e.g., $Q_{\text{actual}} = Q_{\text{base}} \cdot (1 + r_{c,c'})$ where $r_{c,c'}$ is a factor due to the change between crops $c$ and $c'$ during a rotation), while others use additive objective penalties as we do here \cref{eq:temporal benefit}\cite{cai2011dynamic, haneveld2005crop}.
    
    \item \textbf{Exponential vs. linear response:} Process-based crop models like APSIM use exponential growth functions where rotation effects compound over time \cite{keating2003overview}, whereas optimization models typically linearize these effects for tractability \cite{liang2023designing}.
    
    \item \textbf{Magnitude quantification:} Meta-analyses report rotation benefits ranging from 16-23\% yield increase \cite{mudare2025crop, zhao2022global}, but individual studies vary widely depending on region, crop pair, and measurement methodology. Preissel et al.\ \cite{preissel2015magnitude} found legume pre-crop benefits of 0.5-1.6 t/ha for subsequent cereals, while Kirkegaard and Ryan \cite{kirkegaard2014magnitude} documented persistent effects spanning 2-3 growing seasons.
    
    \item \textbf{Spatial interaction treatment:} Traditional approaches impose hard constraints forbidding same-family crops on adjacent plots \cite{ballot2023first}, while ecological studies question whether spatial concentration actually worsens pest impacts or not \cite{karp2018crop, root1973organization}. We use a soft penalty approach (see \cref{eq:synergy}) to place ourselves in between.
    
    \item \textbf{Constraint structure:} Some models enforce rotation through explicit temporal constraints (enforcing some specific type of sequence like 'legume, vegetable, fruit'), others through objective function penalties, and still others through eligibility sets that pre-filter valid crop sequences \cite{haneveld2005crop}.
\end{itemize}


The objective function combines five components: (1) a \textbf{base benefit} term representing intrinsic crop value weighted by farm area; (2) a \textbf{temporal synergy} term modeling crop rotation effects on the same farm over consecutive periods; (3) a \textbf{spatial synergy} term modeling cross-farm interactions between neighboring plots in the same period; (4) a \textbf{one-hot penalty} enforcing single-crop assignment per farm-period; and (5) a \textbf{diversity bonus} rewarding crop variety across the planning horizon.

\subparagraph{Temporal Rotation Effects}
The temporal synergy term captures agronomic interactions through a rotation matrix $R \in \mathbb{R}^{|C| \times |C|}$ that encodes the benefit increase/decrease for each crop pair sequence:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Monoculture penalty} ($c = c'$): Continuous cultivation depletes specific nutrients and builds up soil pathogens.
    \item \textbf{Disease carryover}: Pathogens persist in soil when crops from the same botanical family are grown in succession (e.g., tomato $\to$ potato with late blight).
    \item \textbf{Allelopathy}: Some plants chemically inhibit others through root exudates or decomposing residues.
    \item \textbf{Beneficial rotations}: Nitrogen-fixing legumes improve soil fertility for subsequent grain crops through biological nitrogen fixation.
\end{itemize}

\subparagraph{Spatial Adjacency Effects}
The spatial synergy term models cross-farm ecological interactions on a grid where farms interact with their $k=4$ nearest neighbors:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Positive effects:} Pollination services, beneficial insect sharing, wind breaks, microclimate modification.
    \item \textbf{Negative effects:} Pest and disease spread across farm boundaries, resource competition (water, light).
\end{itemize}





\paragraph{Calibration of Model Parameters}

The rotation synergy matrix $R$ and spatial interaction terms require careful calibration to balance agronomic realism with computational tractability. We ground our parameter choices in empirical agricultural research and established optimization modeling practices.

\textbf{Rotation Synergy Effects.}
Our rotation benefits are calibrated to match empirical findings from large-scale meta-analyses. Recent synthesis of 3,663 paired field-trial observations across six continents shows that crop rotations increase subsequent crop yields by 16-23\% on average compared to monoculture, with legume pre-crops providing the largest benefits \cite{mudare2025crop}. We model these effects through positive entries in $R_{c,c'}$ for beneficial crop sequences, drawn from $\text{Unif}(0.02, 0.20)$.

For monoculture penalties, we set $R_{c,c} = -\beta \cdot 1.5$ with the fixed default $\beta = -0.8$, yielding an effective monoculture penalty of $-1.2$. With rotation weight $\gamma_{\text{rot}} = 0.2$, this produces an objective penalty of approximately 24\% for continuous monoculture, consistent with documented economic disadvantages \cite{preissel2015magnitude, reckling2018grain}.

\textbf{Spatial Adjacency Calibration.}
Traditional rotation planning imposes hard constraints forbidding same-family crops on adjacent plots \cite{ballot2023first}, based on the resource concentration hypothesis \cite{root1973organization}. However, recent large-scale empirical studies found that pest severity is often independent of field size \cite{karp2018crop}. Our soft penalty approach ($0.5 \times 0.3 = 0.15$ of temporal effects) bridges this conflicting evidence while enabling calibration through tunable parameters.

\textbf{Temporal Horizon.}
Our three-period planning horizon ($T = 3$) captures the primary rotation cycle for most cropping systems (e.g., corn-soybean-wheat) while remaining computationally tractable \cite{haneveld2005crop}.

Our formulation uses a \emph{linear} objective contribution for rotation effects, not an exponential yield multiplier \cite{cai2011dynamic}: a monoculture diagonal value of $-1.2$, scaled by $\gamma_{\text{rot}}=0.2$, contributes $-0.24$ to the objective per unit area in consecutive periods — approximately a 24\% penalty relative to the base benefit, consistent with documented economic disadvantages of continuous monoculture \cite{preissel2015magnitude, kirkegaard2014magnitude}.


\paragraph{Decision Variables}
Let $Y_{p,c,t}$ be a binary variable:
\[
    Y_{p,c,t} = \begin{cases} 
    1 & \text{if crop } c \in C \text{ is planted on plot } p \in \mathcal{P} \text{ in period } t \in T \\
    0 & \text{otherwise}
    \end{cases}
\]

\paragraph{Objective Function}

The objective is to maximize the total normalized benefit:
\begin{equation}
\text{maximize} \quad \left( \text{Benefit} + \text{Temporal} + \text{Spatial} - \text{Penalty} \right)
\end{equation}

\subparagraph{Base Benefit (Linear)}
\begin{equation}
\text{Benefit} = \sum_{p \in \mathcal{P}} \sum_{c \in C} \sum_{t \in T} \frac{B_c \cdot L_p}{A_{\text{total}}} \cdot Y_{p,c,t}
\label{eq:temporal benefit}
\end{equation}

\subparagraph{Temporal Synergy (Quadratic)}
\begin{equation}
\text{Temporal} = \gamma_{\text{rot}} \sum_{p \in \mathcal{P}} \sum_{t=2}^{T} \sum_{c \in C} \sum_{c' \in C} \frac{L_p}{A_{\text{total}}} \cdot R_{c,c'} \cdot Y_{p,c,t-1} \cdot Y_{p,c',t}
\label{eq:synergy}
\end{equation}

The rotation matrix $R \in \mathbb{R}^{|C| \times |C|}$ is constructed entry-by-entry with a fixed random seed as:
\begin{equation}
R_{c,c'} = \begin{cases}
\beta \cdot 1.5 & \text{if } c = c' \text{ (monoculture diagonal)} \\
\text{Unif}(1.2\,\beta,\; 0.3\,\beta) & \text{with prob. } p_{\text{comp}} \text{ (disease/competition)} \\
\text{Unif}(0.02, 0.20) & \text{with prob. } 1-p_{\text{comp}} \text{ (beneficial rotation)}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:R_matrix}
\end{equation}
where $\beta = -0.8$ and $p_{\text{comp}} = 0.70$. Substituting these values gives: $R_{c,c} = -1.2$, disease/competition entries $\sim \text{Unif}(-0.96, -0.24)$ (negative, since $\beta < 0$), and beneficial entries $\sim \text{Unif}(0.02, 0.20)$ (positive).

\subparagraph{Spatial Synergy (Quadratic)}
\begin{equation}
\text{Spatial} = \gamma_{\text{spat}} \sum_{(p_1, p_2) \in \mathcal{E}} \sum_{t \in T} \sum_{c \in C} \sum_{c' \in C} \frac{S_{c,c'}}{A_{\text{total}}} \cdot Y_{p_1,c,t} \cdot Y_{p_2,c',t}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{E}$ is the edge set of the $k$-nearest neighbor graph and $S_{c,c'} = 0.3 \cdot R_{c,c'}$.

\subparagraph{One-Hot Penalty (Quadratic)}
\begin{equation}
\text{Penalty} = \lambda_{oh} \sum_{p \in \mathcal{P}} \sum_{t \in T} \left( \left(\sum_{c \in C} Y_{p,c,t}\right) -1 \right)^2
\end{equation}

This soft penalty term penalizes assignments of more or fewer than one crop per plot-period. In the full MIQP (Gurobi), the hard constraints $1 \le \sum_{c} Y_{p,c,t} \le 2$ are additionally enforced as explicit linear inequalities. Rotation violations (same crop in consecutive periods) are checked diagnostically post-solve and are not added as hard constraints in the MIQP.

\subparagraph{Hard Assignment Bounds (Linear)}
In the solved MIQP, plot-period assignment feasibility is enforced by explicit linear constraints:
\begin{equation}
1 \le \sum_{c \in C} Y_{p,c,t} \le 2 \qquad \forall p \in \mathcal{P},\; t \in T
\end{equation}

\begin{itemize}

    \item $\gamma_{\text{spat}} = 0.1 = 0.5\,\gamma_{\text{rot}}$. Spatial interactions between neighboring plots are set at half the temporal coupling strength. This reflects the empirical observation that within-plot temporal effects (soil chemistry, pathogen buildup) are typically stronger than across-fence effects \cite{karp2018crop}.

    \item $S_{c,c'} = 0.3 \cdot R_{c,c'}$. The spatial synergy matrix is the rotation matrix dampened by 0.3. Combined with $\gamma_{\text{spat}} = 0.1$, the effective spatial coupling per unit area is $0.1 \times 0.3 = 0.03$ of the rotation matrix magnitude — approximately 15\% of the within-plot temporal effect.

    \item $\beta = -0.8$, $p_{\text{comp}} = 0.70$. The rotation matrix is predominantly negative: 70\% of off-diagonal entries are disease/competition penalties drawn from $\text{Unif}(-0.96, -0.24)$, and the diagonal (monoculture) entries are fixed at $-1.2$. Only 30\% of off-diagonal pairs are positive synergies. This creates a frustrated optimization landscape analogous to spin-glass problems \cite{lucas2014ising}, testing the solver's ability to navigate conflicting objectives.

    \item $\lambda_{oh} = 3.0$. The one-hot penalty must dominate the $O(1)$ magnitude of benefit and rotation terms to disfavor allocating more than one crop per plot-period. A value of 3.0 is approximately $3\times$ the maximum achievable objective benefit per plot-period, ensuring the penalty is effective without making the landscape numerically unstable.


    \item $k = 4$. Each plot interacts with its 4 nearest neighbors on a synthetic integer grid. This number gives a locally connected but not fully connected spatial graph, keeping the MIQP tractable while modeling ecologically meaningful neighbor effects.

    \item $T = 3$. Three periods is the minimum horizon that captures two consecutive-period rotation transitions (periods 1--2 and 2--3). Classical rotation cycles (e.g., corn--soybean--wheat) are also 3-year, making this the natural choice \cite{haneveld2005crop}. Longer horizons increase problem size quadratically in the temporal terms.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Chain Break Analysis and Mitigation}
We observed chain break rates consistently below 2\% across all problem instances, indicating that the auto-scaled chain strength was effective. This result is significant for several reasons. Chain breaks occur when physical qubits within a chain (representing a single logical variable) disagree in their final state, directly compromising solution validity and causing information loss during quantum annealing~\cite{Cho08}. Published benchmarks on embedding quality report chain break rates of 5--15\% for moderately dense optimization problems~\cite{cai2014practical, zbinden2020embedding}, with rates exceeding 10\% typically indicating severe degradation of solution quality to near-random performance~\cite{VAL16}. Our sub-2\% rate places our embeddings in the high-fidelity regime, comparable to simple tree-like problem structures despite working with constrained quadratic optimization. This performance stems from three factors: (1)~effective automatic chain strength tuning at 1.2--1.8$\times$ maximum quadratic coefficient, balancing thermal stability against objective landscape preservation; (2)~Pegasus topology advantages with native clique sizes of 15--20 qubits and average qubit degree of 15~\cite{BBRR20}, enabling our 27-variable plot subproblems to achieve average chain lengths $\leq 1.2$ (most logical variables require only 1--2 physical qubits); and (3)~strategic problem decomposition creating sparse subproblems naturally compatible with hardware connectivity. The chain break probability for a $k$-qubit chain with single-qubit error rate $\varepsilon$ scales as $1-(1-\varepsilon)^k$; for $\varepsilon \approx 1.5\%$ and $k=1.2$, this predicts $\sim$1.8\% chain breaks, matching our empirical observations. Post-processing via greedy descent repair~\cite{CMR14} required less than 100ms per 100-sample batch, adding negligible overhead (<1\% of QPU time) while recovering $>$95\% of the energy quality that would be achieved with perfect chains. This low chain break rate validates that genuine quantum tunneling effects drive our observed performance rather than classical post-processing artifacts, and ensures that QPU time scales linearly ($O(f)$) with problem size rather than being dominated by error correction overhead~\cite{prxquantum2040322}. 

For coordinated decomposition methods requiring boundary consistency, we employed 3 rounds of iterative refinement (add more explaination) between subproblems.

\subsection{Conversion to Quantum-Annealer-Compatible Formats}

To solve on QPU hardware (without hybrid solvers), we convert the CQM constraints to penalty terms, yielding an unconstrained Binary Quadratic Model (BQM). The general transformation is:
\begin{equation}
\min \quad E(Y) = -Z(Y) + \sum_{i} \lambda_i \cdot P_i(Y)
\end{equation}
where $Z(Y)$ is the negated objective (since quantum annealing minimizes), $P_i(Y)$ are quadratic penalty terms for constraint violations, and $\lambda_i$ are penalty weights. The result is expressed in standard BQM form:
\begin{equation}
E(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{i} h_i x_i + \sum_{i<j} J_{ij} x_i x_j
\end{equation}
where $h_i$ are linear biases and $J_{ij}$ are quadratic couplings.

\subsubsection{BQM for Variant A (Binary Crop Allocation)}

For Variant A with decision variables $Y_{f,c}$ and objective $Z$, the BQM components are:

\textbf{Linear biases} (from objective $Z$):
\begin{equation}
h_{(f,c)} = -\frac{L_p \cdot B_c}{A_{\text{total}}}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Quadratic couplings} (from plot assignment constraint):
\begin{equation}
J_{(p,c),(p,c')} = 2\lambda_{\text{oh}} \quad \forall c \neq c'
\end{equation}

The plot assignment constraint $\sum_{c \in \mathcal{C}} Y_{f,c} \leq 1$ becomes the penalty $P(Y) = \lambda_{\text{oh}} \left(\sum_{c} Y_{f,c} -1\right)^2$, which expands to introduce the quadratic couplings above.

\textbf{Problem size:} $n = |\mathcal{F}| \cdot |\mathcal{C}|$ variables with $|\mathcal{F}| \cdot \binom{|\mathcal{C}|}{2}$ quadratic terms.

\subsubsection{BQM for Variant B (Multi-Period Rotation)}

For Variant B with decision variables $Y_{p,c,t}$, the BQM includes additional terms from the Temporal and Spatial synergy components:

\textbf{Linear biases} (from Benefit term):
\begin{equation}
h_{(p,c,t)} = -\frac{B_c \cdot L_p}{A_{\text{total}}} + 2\lambda_{\text{oh}}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Quadratic couplings}:
\begin{align}
J_{(p,c,t),(p,c',t+1)} &= -\gamma_{\text{rot}} \cdot \frac{R_{c,c'} \cdot L_p}{A_{\text{total}}} && \text{(from Temporal)} \\
J_{(p_1,c,t),(p_2,c',t)} &= -\gamma_{\text{spat}} \cdot \frac{S_{c,c'}}{A_{\text{total}}} && \text{(from Spatial, } (p_1,p_2) \in \mathcal{E} \text{)} \\
J_{(p,c,t),(p,c',t)} &= 2\lambda_{\text{oh}} && \text{(from Penalty, } c \neq c' \text{)}
\end{align}

\textbf{Problem size:} $n = |F| \cdot |C| \cdot |T|$ variables. For a typical instance (5 plots, 6 crop families, 3 periods): $n = 90$ variables with approximately 765 quadratic terms.




\subsubsection{Embedding on Pegasus Topology}

\textbf{Embedding Challenges and Necessity of Problem Decomposition}

The limited connectivity of quantum annealing hardware necessitates embedding logical problem graphs onto the physical qubit topology. While D-Wave's Pegasus architecture offers improved connectivity compared to its predecessor Chimera topology, significant embedding challenges remain for densely connected optimization problems. Research demonstrates a clear correlation between average chain length and the relative errors of solutions sampled, underscoring embedding quality's critical influence on quantum annealing performance~\cite{prxquantum2040322}.

Recent studies establish clear thresholds beyond which direct embedding becomes infeasible, making decomposition strategies essential:

\textbf{Direct Embedding Limitations:}
The embedding of fully connected graphs incurs a quadratic space overhead and thus a significant overhead in the time to solution~\cite{prxquantum2040322,qute202300104}. For Pegasus topology, while algorithms like Clique-Based MinorMiner can embed cliques up to approximately 185 nodes, standard MinorMiner fails to embed even medium-density graphs on 175-180 nodes~\cite{zbinden2020embedding,qute202300104}. For a Pegasus graph with 100\% yield, the largest embeddable complete graph is $K_{150}$ with chain length of 14~\cite{boothby2020next}, though typical QPUs achieve only $\sim$95\% yield, further reducing embeddable problem sizes.

Zbinden et al.~\cite{zbinden2020embedding} systematically evaluated embedding algorithms across different problem densities, finding that direct embedding success rates drop dramatically for graphs with edge densities exceeding 0.2 and node counts above 180. Our plot-level problems with 150+ variables and high connectivity (representing constraints between multiple plots, crops, and time periods) fall well outside this direct embedding success range.

\textbf{Time-to-Solution Overhead:}
Large-scale quantum Monte Carlo simulations suggest that embedding schemes impose a polynomial time-to-solution overhead~\cite{prxquantum2040322}, with large penalty terms tending to affect quantum annealing hardware performance, though weak constraints risk chain breaks that lose logical information~\cite{choi2008minor}. This fundamental trade-off between constraint strength and solution quality further motivates decomposition approaches that reduce problem complexity.

The BQM must be embedded onto the physical qubit connectivity graph. D-Wave's MinorMiner algorithm~\cite{cai2014practical} finds a minor embedding by mapping logical variables to chains of physical qubits. \textbf{Chain strength} couples chained qubits to ensure they remain in the same state during annealing.

\textbf{Key Metrics:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Embedding Time:} Classical overhead for finding the embedding (typically 0.1 to 100 seconds depending on problem connectivity). The HUBO formulation performs slightly better than augmented Lagrangian methods for constrained problems, but its scalability in terms of embeddability in the quantum chip is worse~\cite{qute202300104}, highlighting the importance of formulation choice on embedding feasibility.
    \item \textbf{Chain Length:} Average number of physical qubits per logical variable (lower is better; native cliques have chain length 1). Variables with more or larger couplings require stronger constraints than those with fewer couplings, and one wants to use constraints as small as possible because large penalty terms affect performance~\cite{choi2008minor}.
    \item \textbf{Chain Break Rate:} Fraction of samples where chained qubits disagree (should be $<$2\% for reliable results). Minor embedding introduces new types of errors due to its approximate nature, and quantum annealing correction schemes can improve performance by efficiently decoding errors when their density does not exceed the per-site percolation threshold~\cite{vinci2016nested}.
\end{itemize}

In the Global South, plot sizes follow a highly skewed distribution: approximately 84\% of plots are smaller than 2 hectares, operating only 12\% of total agricultural land \cite{lowder2016number, lowder2021farms}. Among these, plots smaller than 1 hectare constitute about 72\% of all holdings \cite{fao2014farms}. In contrast, plots larger than 20 hectares represent less than 10\% of holdings but occupy the majority of agricultural land, with the largest 1\% of plots controlling over 70\% of global farmland \cite{lowder2021farms}. Our sampling procedure captures this heterogeneity, ensuring that problem instances reflect the diversity of real-world farming landscapes observed in regions such as Sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, and Latin America \cite{samberg2016subnational}.

Plots are assigned spatial coordinates on a 2D grid, and the neighbor graph is constructed by connecting each plot to its 4 nearest neighbors using Euclidean distance. This spatial structure is essential for modeling realistic agricultural interactions such as pest dispersal \cite{garzon2022methodological}, pollination services \cite{ponisio2015spatial, rahimi2021estimating}, and shared infrastructure. The k-nearest neighbor graph construction approach is widely used in spatial agricultural modeling \cite{zhang2024efficient, finley2006forest} and captures essential properties of agricultural landscapes, including local clustering and limited long-range connections \cite{cranmer2012landscape}. The resulting neighbor graph has topological properties similar to real agricultural landscapes, where spatial proximity determines ecological interactions and resource sharing patterns \cite{moreira2018plant, chacon2015landscape}.

\subsection{Classical Solver performance on bigger instances}


The crop allocation problem admits two fundamentally different formulations with distinct computational characteristics:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Farm-level MILP (continuous $A_{fc}$):} Uses continuous allocation fractions $A_{fc} \in [0,1]$ representing the proportion of farm $f$ allocated to crop $c$, with binary indicators $U_c$ for crop selection. This formulation has approximately $54 \times |\mathcal{F}|$ variables (27 continuous $A_{fc}$ + 27 binary $Y_{fc}$ per farm, plus global $U_c$ indicators).
    
    \item \textbf{Binary Plot (BP):} Discretizes each plot into fixed-area plots, each assigned to exactly one crop via binary variables $Y_{p,c} \in \{0,1\}$. This formulation has $27 \times |\mathcal{P}|$ binary variables where $|\mathcal{P}|$ is the number of plots.
\end{itemize}

Before going into the three studies we benchmark both formulations under two solver configurations to see where variable count becomes an issue for Gurobi:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Without proof (MILPGap=1\%):} Allows early termination when solution is within 1\% of optimal bound -- suitable for practical applications where near-optimal solutions suffice.
    \item \textbf{With proof (MILPGap=0\%):} Requires provable global optimality with zero gap -- demonstrates worst-case solver behavior for certification applications.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Key observations:}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Variable count difference:} The MILP formulation has approximately $2\times$ more variables than BP for the same number of plots (e.g., 632,421 vs 316,224 at 11,711 plots) because it includes both continuous $A_{fc}$ and binary $Y_{fc}$ variables. Despite this, MILP solves faster due to efficient LP relaxation.
    
    \item \textbf{Scaling divergence:} At small scales ($<$1,000 plots), both formulations solve in under 0.2 seconds. However, at 74,073 plots, BP takes 376 seconds compared to 8.4 seconds for MILP -- a \textbf{45$\times$ slowdown}. At 148,147 plots, BP fails entirely while MILP completes in 14 seconds.
    
    \item \textbf{Proof overhead:} The ``with proof'' mode (MILPGap=0\%) shows minimal overhead for MILP but can significantly affect BP at large scales. Interestingly, BP with proof sometimes solves faster than without proof (e.g., 264s vs 376s at 74,073 plots) due to different solver heuristics being triggered.
    
    \item \textbf{Infeasibility at small scales:} The BP formulation is infeasible at 3 plots because the discrete plot structure cannot satisfy minimum area constraints that the continuous MILP formulation handles naturally.
\end{enumerate}

The Farm-level MILP formulation benefits from Gurobi's aggressive presolve, which eliminates most binary variables through LP relaxation (the continuous $A_{fc}$ variables dominate the solve). The binary plot formulation faces exponential worst-case complexity due to the combinatorial explosion of discrete choices. This fundamental complexity barrier explains why QUBO-converted problems become computationally prohibitive at scale, motivating our investigation of quantum annealing approaches.

\subsection{Summary of Results}

The comprehensive benchmark results establish several key findings:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Formulation-dependent advantage:} Classical solvers dominate on structured MILP (Variant A), while QPU excels on QUBO/rotation problems (Variant B)

    \item \textbf{Classical Track-A baseline is now objective-aligned:} after removing diversity from the full Gurobi baseline, Study~2.B compares methods in the same MIQP objective family for the classical tracks (full, Clique, SpatialTemporal-5).

    \item \textbf{Gurobi full has a clear scale threshold at 600s:} it is optimal up to 200 plots and then times out at 500+ plots; decomposed Gurobi remains substantially faster at large sizes.

    \item \textbf{QPU and classical objectives must be interpreted by role:} QPU curves are informative for trend and scaling, but not strict MIQP parity, due to decomposition and QUBO-penalty effects.

    \item \textbf{Pure QPU time remains a small runtime share:} the decomposition studies still indicate that embedding/orchestration dominates end-to-end wall time.

    \item \textbf{Embedding is the bottleneck:} Future hardware improvements in qubit connectivity would dramatically improve end-to-end performance
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Synthesis of Key Findings}
% =============================================================================

The three studies presented in Section~\ref{sec:results} collectively establish a nuanced picture: quantum \textit{improvement} is not universal but is \textit{formulation-dependent} and \textit{scale-dependent}. We discuss each study's contribution in turn, followed by an analysis of hardware considerations and future directions.

% FIRST HALF: Study-by-study conclusions

\subsubsection{Study 1.A: Hybrid Solvers -- Performance Without Transparency}

Study 1.A.A (Section~\ref{subsec:hybrid_benchmarks}) established that classical Gurobi consistently finds optimal solutions in under 1.2~seconds across all problem scales (10 to 1,000 plots), confirming its dominance on structured MILP formulations. D-Wave's LeapHybridCQMSolver, by comparison, maintains a constant time profile of approximately 5.3 to 5.5~seconds, due to D-Wave's minimum time execution constraint on jobs. At larger scales (200+ plots), the hybrid CQM solver becomes infeasible, producing solutions with coverage exceeding available land by 15--17$\times$ (Table~\ref{tab:study1_full_results}). This indicates that the hybrid solver's internal heuristics prioritize objective optimization over strict constraint satisfaction when problem density increases -- a trade-off that is invisible to the user and contrasts with Gurobi's consistently feasible solutions.

Beyond the quality gap, the most consequential finding from Study 1.A.A is the minimal QPU contribution within the hybrid solver. Post-hoc analysis revealed that actual QPU annealing time is consistently 35--70\,ms, constituting only \textbf{1.3\%} of total wall-clock time (Figure~\ref{fig:hybrid_solver_performance}). The remaining 98.7\% is classical preprocessing -- problem decomposition, embedding search, and solution refinement. This opacity motivated the development of transparent decomposition methods in Study 2.A, where quantum and classical contributions are explicitly separated and measured.

Study 1.A.B demonstrated that the LeapHybridBQMSolver (Table~\ref{tab:study1_full_results}) successfully solved QUBO-encoded problems across all scales (up to 55,310 variables), whereas classical Gurobi consistently timed out or returned infeasible solutions on the same formulation (Table~\ref{tab:gurobi_qubo_degradation}). This early indication that problem encoding determines solver effectiveness foreshadows the formulation-dependent quantum \textit{improvement} observed in Study 2.B.

\subsubsection{Study 2.A: QPU Decomposition -- Transparent Scaling}

Study 2.A (Section~\ref{subsec:qpu_decomposition}) addressed the black-box limitation by developing transparent decomposition strategies. The central finding is that \textbf{QPU annealing time scales linearly} with problem size: $T_{\text{QPU}} = O(f)$. As shown in Table~\ref{tab:qpu_time_scaling}, QPU time remains under 30~seconds even at 1,000~plots, while classical embedding consumes roughly 76--86\% of total runtime.

The solution quality comparison at 1,000~plots (Table~\ref{tab:quality_1000plots}) reveals a quality-feasibility-diversity trade-off across decomposition strategies. Coordinated decomposition achieves the best objective (31.8\% gap) but introduces 23~constraint violations. Multilevel(10) achieves 39.9\% gap with zero violations and uses all 27~crops -- maximum diversity. By contrast, Gurobi allocates 99.6\% of land to spinach, satisfying diversity constraints minimally.

The violation healing analysis (Section~\ref{subsec:qpu_decomposition}) shows that a meaningful part of apparent raw advantage in some methods is violation-driven. Methods such as CQM-First maintain zero violations across full-span runs, while Coordinated's violations grow with scale. The comprehensive benchmark results (Figures~\ref{fig:qpu_benchmark_small},~\ref{fig:qpu_benchmark_large}, and~\ref{fig:qpu_benchmark_comprehensive}) indicate that Multilevel(10) and CQM-First provide the most stable feasibility-aware trade-off across scales.

\subsubsection{Study 2.B: Variant~B -- Decomposition Versus Monolithic Scaling}

After re-running the classical baseline with diversity removed, Study~2.B now reflects a fairer classical comparison for the 27-crop Track~A objective. The refreshed 600s sweep shows a clear threshold: full Gurobi is optimal through 200 plots, then times out for 500 plots and above (Table~\ref{tab:study2b_three_group}).

The three-group analysis (Figure~\ref{fig:study2b_time_a}) indicates that decomposed Gurobi remains the fastest classical option at larger scales, while full Gurobi becomes timeout-limited. QPU remains valuable as a complementary trend line on decomposed/QUBO-style objectives, but should not be interpreted as direct MIQP parity in absolute value (Figure~\ref{fig:study2b_qual_a}).

The runtime structure remains consistent with Study~2.A: embedding and orchestration are still the dominant bottlenecks in quantum-assisted pipelines, reinforcing that end-to-end performance is currently systems-limited rather than purely anneal-time-limited.

\subsubsection{Cross-Study Conclusions}

Three patterns emerge consistently across all studies:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Quantum \textit{improvement} is formulation-dependent.} Classical Gurobi dominates on Variant~A's structured MILP (Study 1.A.A), while Variant~B exhibits a decomposition-versus-timeout trade-off: monolithic full Gurobi becomes timeout-limited at larger sizes, whereas decomposed methods and QPU-assisted approaches remain tractable. Quantum \textit{improvement} therefore appears in specific objective/encoding regimes rather than as a universal effect~\cite{barahona1982computational, lucasIsingFormulationsMany2014}.

    \item \textbf{Classical embedding is the dominant bottleneck.} In Study 1.A, QPU time is a small fraction of hybrid wall-clock time. In Study 2.A, embedding consumes roughly 76--86\% of total time at scale (Table~\ref{tab:qpu_time_scaling}). In Study 2.B, the refreshed three-group plots retain the same qualitative behavior: end-to-end runtime is dominated by classical orchestration around quantum calls. The QPU scaling ($O(f)$, linear) is already favorable; the bottleneck is the classical embedding step, which grows as $\sim O(f^{1.5})$~\cite{zbinden2020embedding}.

    \item \textbf{Decomposition methods produce practically valuable solutions.} Quantum decomposition yields diverse crop allocations (all 27~crops in Multilevel(10)) compared to Gurobi's monoculture optimum (99.6\% spinach, Table~\ref{tab:quality_1000plots}). A sensitivity analysis over 10,000~random weight configurations confirms that this monoculture outcome is a robust property of the benefit score structure rather than an artefact of a single weight vector (Appendix~\ref{app:sensitivity}). For agricultural planning, this diversity provides resilience, nutritional variety, and soil health benefits that raw objective value does not capture~\cite{lin2011resilience, bowles2020longterm}. Where constraint violations occur (Study 2.A: Table~\ref{tab:violation_by_strategy}; Study 2.B: Figure~\ref{fig:study2b_healed_violations}), their nature -- fields left temporarily fallow or rotation soft-penalty violations -- is addressable in post-processing and may in part reflect deliberate soft-constraint design choices~\cite{dury2012cropping, hobbs2008conservation}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{QPU Noise and Chain Break Management}

The D-Wave Advantage system operates as a noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) device~\cite{preskillQuantumComputingNISQ2018, boothby2020next}, subject to thermal fluctuations, control errors, and inter-qubit coupling imperfections. The primary hardware artifact in our experiments was \textbf{chain breaks}: when a logical variable requires multiple physical qubits (a chain), thermal noise can cause disagreement among chained qubits~\cite{choi2008minor}.

Our decomposition strategy was explicitly designed to minimize this effect. Plot-level subproblems (27~variables) achieve chain lengths $\leq 1.2$ on the Pegasus topology~\cite{boothby2020next}, and native clique embeddings (15--20 qubits fully connected) exhibited zero chain breaks. Across all experiments, the chain break rate remained below 2\%, with auto-scaled chain strength (1.2--1.8$\times$ the maximum quadratic coefficient) proving consistently effective~\cite{venturelli2015quantum}. Automatic postprocessing via greedy descent~\cite{cai2014practical} adds negligible time ($<$100\,ms per sample) and repairs any remaining chain break artifacts.

The observable effect of noise was sample variance: the 100~samples per QPU call exhibited a distribution of objective values rather than converging to a single solution. This is expected behavior for quantum annealing -- the sampler explores a neighborhood around the minimum-energy state -- and is addressed by selecting the best sample from each batch. For future work, adaptive annealing schedules~\cite{marshall2019power, king2022coherent} and reverse annealing~\cite{venturelli2019reverse} -- starting from a classical initial solution and refining via quantum fluctuations -- could improve sampling of low-energy states in the frustrated rotation landscape.

\subsubsection{The Embedding Bottleneck}

The single most important finding across all three studies is that \textbf{classical embedding dominates end-to-end runtime}. Table~\ref{tab:qpu_time_scaling} shows this clearly at full scale: QPU annealing remains in the tens-of-seconds regime, while embedding consumes the majority share of wall-clock time (roughly 76--86\% in the updated full-span benchmark).

Embedding complexity depends on problem connectivity and QPU topology~\cite{choi2008minor, zbinden2020embedding}. Our decomposition strategies explicitly control this trade-off:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{PlotBased:} Sparse per-plot subproblems (27 variables) $\to$ embedding in $<$0.5\,s per partition
    \item \textbf{Multilevel(10):} Medium subproblems ($\sim$270 variables) $\to$ embedding in 5--30\,s per partition
    \item \textbf{Direct QPU:} Full problem (27,027 variables) $\to$ embedding failure (Table~\ref{tab:quality_1000plots})
\end{itemize}

This hierarchy establishes a design principle: decomposition strategies should create subproblems that match hardware capabilities. For the current Pegasus topology, targeting 20--50 variable partitions with sparse connectivity ensures fast, reliable embedding. Any hardware improvement that reduces embedding overhead -- whether through higher connectivity, larger native cliques, or dedicated embedding co-processors~\cite{boothby2021zephyr} -- would have an outsized impact on end-to-end performance.

If embedding overhead were eliminated entirely, the 1,000-plot problem would complete in the tens-of-seconds regime rather than the current decomposition wall time. This acceleration would place QPU workflows much closer to classical runtimes on Variant~A while substantially improving practical competitiveness on difficult Variant~B timeout regimes.

\subsubsection{Impact of Higher Qubit Connectivity}

The most directly relevant hardware improvement for our application is \textbf{increased qubit connectivity}. The current D-Wave Advantage system uses the Pegasus topology with degree-15 connectivity, supporting native cliques of 15--20 qubits~\cite{boothby2020next}. Our plot-level subproblems (27~variables for 27-Food, 18~variables for 6-Food) can embed within or near these native cliques, but larger subproblems (e.g., the 270-variable partitions in Multilevel(10)) require chains and multi-second embedding searches~\cite{cai2014practical}.

D-Wave's next-generation \textbf{Zephyr topology} offers degree-20+ connectivity, supporting native cliques of approximately 30--40 qubits~\cite{boothby2021zephyr}. For our problem, this advancement would have three concrete effects:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Larger chain-free subproblems:} Plot clusters of 5--7~plots (up to $\sim$126 variables for 6-Food, $\sim$189 for 27-Food) could embed without chains, reducing the number of partitions by 5--7$\times$ and proportionally reducing both QPU calls and coordination overhead.

    \item \textbf{Faster embedding for remaining chains:} Higher connectivity reduces the search space for MinorMiner~\cite{cai2014practical}, decreasing per-partition embedding time from the current 5--30\,s to projections of $<$1\,s for moderate subproblems.

    \item \textbf{Reduced boundary approximation error:} Fewer, larger partitions mean fewer inter-partition boundaries, reducing the decomposition approximation that accounts for $\sim$93\% of the current optimality gap. This could close the 30--40\% optimality gaps observed in Study 2.A to substantially lower values.
\end{enumerate}

Combining these effects, we conservatively estimate that Zephyr-class connectivity could yield a 5--10$\times$ end-to-end speedup for the decomposition methods benchmarked in Study 2.A, and could reduce optimality gaps by 30--50\% through improved boundary coordination.

\subsubsection{Impact of Larger QPU Processors and Lower Noise}

Beyond connectivity, future QPUs with more qubits would enable embedding larger subproblems -- or even entire problems -- without decomposition. Current Pegasus systems have $\sim$5,760 qubits~\cite{boothby2020next}; next-generation systems may offer 10,000--20,000+ qubits~\cite{boothby2021zephyr}. For our 200-plot 27-Food rotation problem (16,200 variables), a QPU with sufficient qubits and connectivity could eliminate decomposition entirely, removing boundary approximation errors, solving the global optimization in a single annealing call ($\sim$20\,ms), and producing solutions that respect global constraints natively.

Improved qubit coherence and reduced thermal noise would further benefit our application. Our current chain break rate of $<$2\% is already low, but further reduction to $<$0.1\% would enable more aggressive penalty tuning. Currently, chain strength must be set conservatively (1.2--1.8$\times$ max coefficient) to prevent breaks, which can distort the energy landscape. Lower noise would allow tighter chain strengths, producing more faithful representations of the QUBO objective and potentially improving solution quality. Additionally, reduced sample variance would increase the probability that the best sample from each batch is close to the true ground state -- particularly impactful for problems with many nearly-degenerate local minima, such as our frustrated rotation landscapes.

\subsubsection{Parallelization and Multi-QPU Architectures}

Our decomposition approach is inherently parallelizable: independent plot clusters can be solved simultaneously on separate QPUs. For the 1,000-plot Multilevel(10) benchmark (102~partitions), a hypothetical 10-QPU array would reduce QPU time from 21.78\,s to $\sim$2.2\,s. The embedding bottleneck would also parallelize: each QPU independently embeds its assigned partitions, reducing embedding wall-clock time by a factor of 10.

Under such a parallel architecture, the 1,000-plot problem currently requiring $\sim$3,495\,s would complete in approximately $\sim$350\,s -- comparable to a single Gurobi timeout, but delivering a solution rather than hitting a time limit. Combined with Zephyr-class connectivity improvements, parallel multi-QPU execution could bring the 1,000-plot Variant~A problem to completion in under 100~seconds, and could solve larger Variant~B rotation scenarios in time regimes where monolithic full Gurobi currently times out.

\subsubsection{Conditions for Realistic Quantum \textit{Improvement}}

Based on the evidence from all three studies, we identify the following conditions under which practical quantum \textit{improvement} could transition from the demonstration presented here to a deployment-ready capability:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Problem structure must favor QUBO encoding.} Quantum \textit{improvement} emerges specifically for problems where quadratic coupling (rotation constraints, synergy terms) creates frustrated landscapes~\cite{barahona1982computational, lucasIsingFormulationsMany2014} that are not solvable via classical branch-and-bound. On Variant~A's structured MILP, Gurobi solves in $<$1.2\,s at all scales (Section~\ref{subsec:hybrid_benchmarks}); on Variant~B's MIQP rotation formulation, timeout behavior depends strongly on formulation and scale (Table~\ref{tab:gurobi_struggles}).

    \item \textbf{Classical embedding overhead must be reduced.} Hardware improvements in connectivity (Zephyr and beyond)~\cite{boothby2021zephyr}, algorithmic improvements to MinorMiner~\cite{cai2014practical}, or the development of topology-aware problem formulations could each contribute to reducing the embedding overhead that currently dominates runtime in decomposition workflows (Table~\ref{tab:qpu_time_scaling})~\cite{zbinden2020embedding}.

    \item \textbf{Decomposition quality must improve.} The 30--40\% optimality gaps observed in Study 2.A are acceptable for many practical applications but leave room for improvement. Larger native cliques, better boundary coordination algorithms, and hardware-aware decomposition strategies co-designed with QPU topology could close this gap substantially.

    \item \textbf{Scale must be sufficient.} In Study~2.B, the refreshed three-group analysis shows a clear size threshold where monolithic full Gurobi becomes timeout-limited while decomposed classical and QPU-assisted pipelines continue to return solutions. Quantum \textit{improvement} is therefore projected to be most relevant at larger regional planning scales (1,000--10,000 plots), where decomposition and orchestration strategy dominate runtime behavior.
\end{enumerate}

These conditions are not speculative: they are directly derived from the quantitative findings of the three studies. The gap between current demonstration and deployment-ready \textit{improvement} is primarily an engineering challenge (embedding overhead, QPU connectivity) rather than a fundamental theoretical barrier.

\subsubsection{Fault-Tolerant Quantum Computing Considerations}

While our work uses quantum annealing rather than gate-based quantum computing, the broader trajectory toward fault-tolerant quantum computation (FTQC)~\cite{campbell2017roads} has implications for the problem class studied here. Fault-tolerant quantum optimization algorithms (e.g., quantum approximate optimization algorithm variants~\cite{edwardfarhiQuantumApproximateOptimization2014} with error correction) could in principle solve QUBO problems with guaranteed approximation ratios, removing the stochastic variability inherent in current quantum annealing.

However, for the near term, the relevant question is whether \textit{current NISQ-era} quantum annealers can deliver practical \textit{improvement} for our specific problem class. Our results suggest the answer is conditionally affirmative in decomposition-oriented regimes, but the refreshed Study~2.B results also show that conclusions depend on objective family and comparability assumptions. End-to-end wall-clock time remains dominated by classical embedding overhead rather than quantum computation limits.

\subsection{Conclusions}
% =============================================================================

Phase~3 established quantum annealing as a viable computational approach for large-scale agricultural optimization, with demonstrated quantum \textit{improvement} on the crop rotation formulation (Variant B) and clear pathways to enhanced performance through hardware evolution. The key conclusions, each grounded in specific experimental evidence, are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Quantum \textit{improvement} is formulation-dependent.} Classical Gurobi solves Variant~A's structured MILP quickly at all tested scales, while Variant~B exhibits mixed timeout behavior (Table~\ref{tab:gurobi_struggles}): uniform timeout in 6-Food and mostly optimal solves in 27-Food aggregated except the largest case.

    \item \textbf{QPU runtime scaling remains favorable.} Pure QPU time scales approximately linearly with problem size in decomposition workflows, while end-to-end wall time remains dominated by classical embedding and coordination.

    \item \textbf{Classical embedding is the primary bottleneck.} QPU time constitutes only a small share of total runtime in Study~2.A (about 1.2--3.1\% in Table~\ref{tab:qpu_time_scaling}), with embedding search consuming the remainder. This finding identifies the principal target for both hardware improvements (higher connectivity) and algorithmic optimization (topology-aware decomposition).

    \item \textbf{Transparency reveals genuine quantum contribution.} The transition from black-box hybrid solvers (Study 1.A.A) to transparent decomposition methods (Study 2.A) revealed that hybrid solver QPU usage is only 35--70\,ms (1.3\% of wall-clock time), motivating explicit quantum-classical separation. This transparency is essential for honest assessment of quantum contribution and for projecting performance on future hardware.

    \item \textbf{Quantum solutions offer practical diversity benefits.} Decomposition methods naturally produce diverse crop allocations (all 27~crops in Multilevel(10)) compared to Gurobi's monoculture optimum (99.6\% spinach), as documented in Table~\ref{tab:quality_1000plots}. For agricultural planning, this diversity provides resilience, nutritional variety, and soil health benefits that raw objective value does not capture~\cite{lin2011resilience, bowles2020longterm}.

    \item \textbf{Violations are a manageable trade-off.} Zero-violation methods exist for Study 2.A (Table~\ref{tab:violation_by_strategy}), while Study~2.B confirms one-hot feasibility for full Gurobi and highlights that most reported violations are tied to soft rotation penalties and decomposition approximations. These effects are diagnosable and can be mitigated in post-processing.

    \item \textbf{Future hardware improvements target the right bottleneck.} Higher qubit connectivity (Zephyr topology), larger processors, lower noise, and multi-QPU parallelization all directly address the classical embedding overhead that currently dominates runtime. If embedding overhead were eliminated, the 1,000-plot problem would complete in $\sim$22~seconds of QPU time -- a regime where quantum \textit{improvement} is decisive.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{SDG Interlinkages: Synergies, Trade-offs, and Mitigation Strategies}

The quantum optimization approach creates a complex network of SDG interlinkages that must be carefully managed to maximize synergies and minimize trade-offs \cite{Fanzo2022,UNFSS_Planet_2023,Sylvester2024}. Understanding these relationships enables responsible innovation that advances holistic sustainable development rather than optimizing narrow metrics at the expense of broader welfare.

\subsubsection{Primary Synergies}

Several mutually reinforcing pathways amplify the solution's positive impacts across multiple SDGs simultaneously.

The \textbf{nutrition-health-productivity pathway} (SDG 2 $\rightarrow$ SDG 3 $\rightarrow$ SDG 8) creates a virtuous cycle. Optimizing for nutritional density directly improves health outcomes, which in turn enhances workforce productivity and agricultural capacity. This reinforcing loop compounds benefits over time as healthier populations demonstrate greater economic participation and better educational attainment.

The \textbf{poverty-food-economics triangle} interconnects poverty reduction (SDG 1), food security (SDG 2), and economic growth (SDG 8) in a mutually supporting relationship. Agricultural optimization addressing all three simultaneously achieves greater total impact than addressing any single goal in isolation. By maximizing affordability (low cost per unit of nutritional value), the optimizer reduces food expenditure burdens on consumers; combined with dietary diversification, this improves nutritional security while freeing household income for other needs, contributing to local economic activity.

The \textbf{technology-innovation-partnership chain} demonstrates how quantum computing application (SDG 9) strengthens international partnerships (SDG 17) by facilitating cutting-edge technology transfer to developing contexts while building local technical capacity. This pathway enables knowledge sharing, reduces inequalities through accessible technology (SDG 10), and drives economic growth (SDG 8) through innovation adoption.

The \textbf{climate-ecosystem nexus} shows how the environmental optimization objectives simultaneously address multiple environmental SDGs through their underlying LCA damage categories. Specifically: \texttt{Climate\_change\_mPt} directly relates to climate action (SDG~13); \texttt{Land\_use\_mPt} relates to terrestrial ecosystem pressure (SDG~15); \texttt{Water\_use\_mPt} relates to water management (SDG~6); and \texttt{Eutrophication\_mPt} captures nutrient runoff that can degrade aquatic systems (SDG~14). By penalizing crops with high scores in these categories, the optimizer indirectly favors crop mixes with lower aggregate environmental footprints across these dimensions. However, biodiversity protection (SDG~15) is only indirectly supported through reduced land-use pressure; the optimizer does not explicitly model species richness or habitat preservation.

\subsubsection{Critical Trade-offs and Mitigation Strategies}

While the solution generates numerous synergies, several fundamental tensions require active management to prevent negative impacts.

\textbf{Productivity-Environment Tension (SDG 2 vs SDGs 6, 13, 14, 15):}

The fundamental trade-off between maximizing food production and minimizing environmental impact represents the core challenge of sustainable agriculture. Without explicit constraints, optimization might select high-yield crops that deplete water resources, increase GHG emissions, harm terrestrial ecosystems, or generate agricultural runoff degrading aquatic environments.

\textit{Mitigation:} The optimization framework employs explicit multi-objective formulations that balance nutritional adequacy and affordability with environmental sustainability. Environmental impact scores from life-cycle assessments constrain solutions to minimize land use, water consumption, and carbon footprint while maintaining nutritional adequacy. Pareto analysis reveals optimal balance points where multiple objectives coexist rather than compete. Continuous monitoring ensures environmental constraints remain effective as agricultural practices evolve.

\textbf{Technology Access and Digital Divide (SDG 9 vs SDG 10):}

While quantum optimization provides powerful capabilities, it risks exacerbating inequalities if access is limited to well-resourced actors. Technology adoption may inadvertently widen the gap between large commercial operations and smallholder farmers, undermining poverty reduction (SDG 1) and equality (SDG 10) goals even while advancing innovation (SDG 9).

\textit{Mitigation:} Access equity requires deliberate design choices including: open-source software releases ensuring zero-cost availability; user-friendly interfaces requiring minimal technical expertise; partnerships with agricultural extension services providing last-mile technology dissemination to smallholder farmers; community-based facilitator models bridging the digital divide; differentiated support mechanisms for smaller plots; cooperative models enabling resource pooling; and progressive pricing structures favoring disadvantaged farmers. Capacity-building programs ensure technology access translates to effective utilization.

\textbf{Climate Policy Costs (SDG 13 vs SDGs 1, 2, 8):}

Environmental optimization may increase short-term costs for implementing sustainable practices, potentially harming poor households and slowing immediate economic growth. The transition to climate-smart agriculture requires upfront investments that may be prohibitive for resource-constrained farmers, creating tension between climate action and poverty reduction.

\textit{Mitigation:} Balancing climate goals with poverty reduction and food security requires complementary policy interventions: phased implementation allowing gradual adaptation; transition subsidies protecting vulnerable households during the shift to sustainable practices; technical assistance reducing implementation costs through knowledge transfer; market development for sustainable products commanding premium prices; payments for ecosystem services compensating farmers for environmental benefits they generate; social protection systems providing safety nets during transitions; climate-resilient livelihood diversification reducing ploter vulnerability; and staged optimization ensuring affordability remains a weighted objective across all time horizons. Just transition frameworks ensure equitable distribution of costs and benefits.

\textbf{Conservation-Production Balance (SDG 15 vs SDG 2):}

Protecting biodiversity and preventing land degradation may constrain agricultural expansion, creating tension between environmental protection and food security. In land-scarce contexts, preserving natural areas for ecosystem health can limit the area available for food production.

\textit{Mitigation:} Resolution requires landscape-level approaches including: sustainable intensification on existing farmland rather than extensification into natural areas; agroforestry and agroecological systems integrating conservation with production; landscape-level planning designating distinct conservation and production zones; restoration of degraded lands for agricultural use; payment schemes compensating farmers for conservation activities; and demonstrating that soil health improvements and ecosystem services support long-term agricultural productivity, aligning conservation with food security objectives.

\textbf{Economic Growth Without Safeguards (SDG 8 vs SDG 13):}

Agricultural economic growth, if pursued without environmental constraints, may increase emissions and resource consumption. The drive for productivity and income gains could undermine climate goals if environmental impacts are not explicitly managed.

\textit{Mitigation:} The optimization explicitly constrains environmental impacts while maximizing economic value, ensuring growth aligns with sustainability. Long-term strategies include mainstreaming environmental safeguards in agricultural policy, implementing carbon pricing or offset mechanisms, and demonstrating that sustainable intensification supports both growth and climate goals simultaneously.

\textbf{Consumption Patterns and Economic Growth (SDG 12 vs SDG 8):}

The sustainability objective includes \texttt{Waste\_treatment\_mPt} as part of the full-lifecycle environmental assessment, meaning the optimizer indirectly penalizes crops whose post-consumer waste processing has high environmental costs. However, the optimizer does not directly model consumer behavior, food waste rates, or consumption patterns. Claims about responsible consumption are therefore limited to the production side: the optimizer favors crops with lower full-lifecycle environmental damage, which includes the waste treatment stage. Promoting responsible consumption at the consumer level would require complementary interventions beyond the scope of this optimization.

\textit{Mitigation:} Market development for sustainable products commanding premium prices transforms environmental responsibility into economic opportunity. Certification programs add value to sustainably produced crops, while consumer education creates demand. Long-term economic benefits of sustainable practices -- including reduced input costs and climate resilience -- outweigh short-term transition costs.

\textbf{Unequal Partnership Structures (SDG 17 vs SDG 10):}

Multi-stakeholder partnerships, while essential for technology deployment, may reinforce existing power imbalances if not carefully structured. Partnerships dominated by well-resourced institutions could marginalize smallholder ploter voices. Furthermore, quantum computing hardware and cloud services are predominantly provided by technology companies in North America and Europe, which could create technological dependencies for developing countries relying on these platforms for agricultural decision support.

\textit{Mitigation:} Participatory governance structures with ploter representatives in decision-making bodies ensure equitable influence. Transparent benefit-sharing agreements document how gains are distributed. Capacity building programs equalize negotiating power between partners. Regular stakeholder consultations verify that partnership benefits reach intended beneficiaries. To mitigate technological dependency, the optimization framework is designed to also run on classical solvers (Gurobi, SCIP) when quantum access is unavailable, ensuring the solution remains functional independently of quantum cloud providers.

\subsubsection{Monitoring and Adaptive Management}

Responsible innovation requires ongoing assessment of both intended and unintended consequences across the SDG network. The impact framework includes: baseline and endline surveys for all indicator measurements, participatory monitoring engaging farming communities in data collection and interpretation, regular SDG interlinkage assessments using network analysis methods \cite{Fanzo2022} to identify emerging trade-offs or unexpected synergies, and adaptive optimization that adjusts objective weights based on observed outcomes and stakeholder feedback. This adaptive approach ensures the solution remains aligned with holistic sustainable development goals rather than inadvertently creating negative externalities while optimizing for specific metrics.

\subsection{Mapping to D-Wave QPU: Decomposition Strategies for Study 2.A}
\label{appx:decomp}

\textbf{Observation:} For our plot-level subproblems (27 variables), we consistently achieved chain length $\leq 1.2$ and embedding time $<0.5$ seconds, validating the suitability of Pegasus topology for our decomposition strategies. These metrics place our subproblems firmly within the ``sweet spot'' identified by embedding algorithm research, where direct embedding is both feasible and efficient.

\subsubsection{Method 1: Direct Embedding Baseline}

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Direct QPU Embedding}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require CQM with variables $\mathcal{V}$, constraints $\mathcal{C}$
\Ensure Solution $\mathbf{x}$ or failure
\State Convert CQM to BQM: $\text{BQM} \leftarrow \text{cqm\_to\_bqm}(\text{CQM}, \lambda)$
\State Build source graph $G_s = (\mathcal{V}, E_s)$ from BQM quadratic terms
\State Get QPU target graph $G_t = (Q, E_t)$ from Pegasus topology
\State Find embedding: $\phi: \mathcal{V} \rightarrow 2^Q$ using minorminer
\If{embedding found within timeout}
    \State Sample: $\mathbf{x} \leftarrow \text{QPU.sample}(\text{BQM}, \phi, n_{\text{reads}})$
    \State \Return best feasible solution
\Else
    \State \Return FAIL (problem too large)
\EndIf
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\paragraph{Complexity}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Time}: $O(T_{\text{embed}} + n_{\text{reads}} \cdot T_{\text{QPU}})$ where $T_{\text{embed}}$ can be exponential
\item \textbf{Space}: $O(|\mathcal{V}| \cdot c)$ physical qubits, where $c$ is chain length (typically 2-10)
\item \textbf{Limitations}: Fails when $|\mathcal{V}| > 300$-500 or high connectivity
\end{itemize}


\subsubsection{Method 2: Plot-Based Decomposition}

\paragraph{Description}
Plot-based decomposition partitions the problem by plot, creating one subproblem per plot plus a master problem for unique crop tracking. This ensures constraint preservation since plots are independent. This natural domain-aware partitioning exploits the independence structure inherent in agricultural planning, similar to decomposition strategies used in quantum annealing for scheduling~\cite{venturelli2015quantum} and graph coloring problems~\cite{titiloye2011quantum}.

\paragraph{Mathematical Formulation}

Partition variables into plot-specific subsets plus global U variables:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{P}_{\text{PlotBased}} = \{\mathcal{P}_1, \mathcal{P}_2, \ldots, \mathcal{P}_{|\mathcal{F}|}, \mathcal{P}_U\}
\end{equation}

where:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\mathcal{P}_i = \{Y_{f_i,c} : c \in \mathcal{C}\}$ for plot $f_i$
\item $\mathcal{P}_U = \{U_c : c \in \mathcal{C}\}$
\end{itemize}

Each plot partition has $|\mathcal{C}|$ variables (one per crop), creating $|\mathcal{F}| + 1$ partitions.

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Plot-Based Decomposition}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Data with $|\mathcal{F}|$ plots, $|\mathcal{C}|$ crops
\Ensure Complete solution $\mathbf{x}$
\State Create partitions: $\mathcal{P}_f = \{Y_{f,c} : c \in \mathcal{C}\}$ for each plot $f$
\State Create U partition: $\mathcal{P}_U = \{U_c : c \in \mathcal{C}\}$
\State $\mathbf{x} \leftarrow \emptyset$
\For{each partition $\mathcal{P}$ in $\{\mathcal{P}_1, \ldots, \mathcal{P}_{|\mathcal{F}|}, \mathcal{P}_U\}$}
    \State Build BQM for variables in $\mathcal{P}$ with objective and local constraints
    \State Embed BQM on QPU: $\phi_{\mathcal{P}} \leftarrow \text{find\_embedding}(\mathcal{P}, G_t)$
    \State Sample: $\mathbf{x}_{\mathcal{P}} \leftarrow \text{QPU.sample}(\text{BQM}_{\mathcal{P}}, \phi_{\mathcal{P}}, n_{\text{reads}})$
    \State Merge with conflict resolution: $\mathbf{x} \leftarrow \text{merge}(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x}_{\mathcal{P}})$
\EndFor
\State \Return $\mathbf{x}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\paragraph{Conflict Resolution}

When merging partition solutions, plot assignment conflicts are resolved by benefit comparison:

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Conflict Resolution for Plot Assignments}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require New assignment $Y_{f,c} = 1$, existing assignments $\mathbf{x}$
\If{$\exists c' : \mathbf{x}[Y_{f,c'}] = 1$} \Comment{Conflict detected}
    \State $b_{\text{new}} \leftarrow b_c \cdot L_p$
    \State $b_{\text{old}} \leftarrow b_{c'} \cdot L_p$
    \If{$b_{\text{new}} > b_{\text{old}}$}
        \State $\mathbf{x}[Y_{f,c'}] \leftarrow 0$ \Comment{Replace with better option}
        \State $\mathbf{x}[Y_{f,c}] \leftarrow 1$
    \EndIf
\Else
    \State $\mathbf{x}[Y_{f,c}] \leftarrow 1$ \Comment{No conflict}
\EndIf
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\paragraph{Complexity}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Partitions}: $|\mathcal{F}| + 1$
\item \textbf{Partition size}: $|\mathcal{C}|$ variables each
\item \textbf{Total QPU calls}: $|\mathcal{F}| + 1$
\item \textbf{Embedding}: Fast (small partitions)
\item \textbf{Constraint preservation}: Excellent (plots independent)
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Method 3: Multilevel Partitioning}

\paragraph{Description}
Multilevel partitioning groups plots into larger clusters of size $k$, reducing the number of partitions at the cost of partition size and potential constraint violations. This hierarchical approach is inspired by multilevel graph partitioning techniques widely used in VLSI design and parallel computing~\cite{karypis1998fast, hendrickson1995multilevel}.

\paragraph{Mathematical Formulation}

Group plots into clusters of size $k$:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{P}_{\text{Multilevel}} = \{\mathcal{P}_1, \ldots, \mathcal{P}_{\lceil |\mathcal{F}|/k \rceil}, \mathcal{P}_U\}
\end{equation}

where:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{P}_i = \{Y_{f,c} : f \in \mathcal{F}_i, c \in \mathcal{C}\}
\end{equation}

and $\mathcal{F}_i$ is a subset of $k$ plots. Each partition has $k \cdot |\mathcal{C}|$ variables.

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Multilevel Partitioning ($k$-plot groups)}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Data with $|\mathcal{F}|$ plots, group size $k$
\Ensure Solution $\mathbf{x}$
\State Divide plots into groups: $\mathcal{F} = \bigcup_{i=1}^{\lceil |\mathcal{F}|/k \rceil} \mathcal{F}_i$ where $|\mathcal{F}_i| \leq k$
\For{each plot group $\mathcal{F}_i$}
    \State $\mathcal{P}_i \leftarrow \{Y_{f,c} : f \in \mathcal{F}_i, c \in \mathcal{C}\}$
    \State Build BQM for $\mathcal{P}_i$ with one-crop constraints for each $f \in \mathcal{F}_i$
    \State Solve partition on QPU
    \State Merge solution with conflict resolution
\EndFor
\State Solve U partition
\State \Return $\mathbf{x}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\paragraph{Trade-offs}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Fewer partitions}: $\lceil |\mathcal{F}|/k \rceil + 1$ vs $|\mathcal{F}| + 1$
\item \textbf{Larger partitions}: $k \cdot |\mathcal{C}|$ variables vs $|\mathcal{C}|$
\item \textbf{Embedding difficulty}: Increases with $k$
\item \textbf{Violations}: Can occur when plots in the same partitions compete for crops
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Method 4: Louvain Community Detection}

\paragraph{Description}
Louvain decomposition uses community detection on the variable interaction graph to create partitions that minimize cross-partition edges. This is a graph-theoretic approach that adapts to problem structure. The method is based on the fast community detection algorithm by Blondel et al.~\cite{blondel2008fast}, which maximizes modularity to identify densely connected subgraphs in the variable interaction network.

\paragraph{Mathematical Formulation}

Build interaction graph $G_{\text{int}} = (\mathcal{V}, E_{\text{int}})$ where:
\begin{equation}
E_{\text{int}} = \{(Y_{f,c}, Y_{f,c'}) : f \in \mathcal{F}, c \neq c' \in \mathcal{C}\} \cup \{(Y_{f,c}, U_c) : f \in \mathcal{F}, c \in \mathcal{C}\}
\end{equation}

Apply Louvain algorithm to maximize modularity:
\begin{equation}
Q = \frac{1}{2m} \sum_{i,j} \left[ A_{ij} -- \frac{k_i k_j}{2m} \right] \delta(c_i, c_j)
\end{equation}

where $m = |E_{\text{int}}|$, $A$ is adjacency matrix, $k_i$ is degree of node $i$, and $\delta(c_i, c_j) = 1$ if nodes $i,j$ are in same community.

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Louvain Community Detection Decomposition}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Variable interaction graph $G_{\text{int}}$
\Ensure Partition set $\mathcal{P} = \{\mathcal{P}_1, \ldots, \mathcal{P}_n\}$
\State Initialize: each variable in its own community
\Repeat
    \For{each variable $v$}
        \State Find community $C$ that maximizes modularity gain
        \State Move $v$ to $C$ if gain is positive
    \EndFor
    \State Aggregate communities into super-nodes
\Until{no modularity improvement}
\State Split partitions exceeding size limit: $|\mathcal{P}_i| \leq \text{max\_size}$
\State \Return $\mathcal{P}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\paragraph{Characteristics}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Adaptive}: Partition structure follows problem connectivity
\item \textbf{Many partitions}: Typically creates $|\mathcal{F}|$ to $2|\mathcal{F}|$ partitions
\item \textbf{Variable partition sizes}: From 2 to max\_size variables
\item \textbf{Modularity optimization}: Minimizes cross-partition interactions
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Method 5: CQM-First Decomposition}

\paragraph{Description}
CQM-first decomposition partitions at the CQM level before converting to BQM, preserving constraint structure within partitions. This addresses the fundamental issue that BQM-first approaches lose constraint information during penalty encoding~\cite{lucas2014ising}. By maintaining explicit constraints within subproblems, this method improves solution quality compared to standard penalty-based approaches.

\paragraph{Key Innovation}

Standard decomposition: $\text{CQM} \rightarrow \text{BQM} \rightarrow \text{Partition}$

CQM-first: $\text{CQM} \rightarrow \text{Partition} \rightarrow \text{Sub-CQMs} \rightarrow \text{BQMs}$

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{CQM-First Decomposition with Constraint Preservation}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require CQM with variables $\mathcal{V}$, constraints $\mathcal{C}$
\Require Partition function $\Pi: \mathcal{V} \rightarrow \{\mathcal{P}_1, \ldots, \mathcal{P}_n\}$
\Ensure Solution $\mathbf{x}$
\State Partition variables: $\{\mathcal{P}_1, \ldots, \mathcal{P}_n\} \leftarrow \Pi(\mathcal{V})$
\State Identify master partition $\mathcal{P}_U$ containing U variables
\State \textbf{Phase 1: Solve Master}
\State Extract sub-CQM for $\mathcal{P}_U$ with food group constraints
\State Convert to BQM: $\text{BQM}_U \leftarrow \text{cqm\_to\_bqm}(\text{Sub-CQM}_U, \lambda)$
\State $\mathbf{x}_U \leftarrow \text{QPU.sample}(\text{BQM}_U)$
\State \textbf{Phase 2: Solve Subproblems with Fixed U}
\For{partition $\mathcal{P}_i$ where $i \neq U$}
    \State Extract sub-CQM for $\mathcal{P}_i$ with $\mathbf{x}_U$ fixed
    \State Convert to BQM: $\text{BQM}_i \leftarrow \text{cqm\_to\_bqm}(\text{Sub-CQM}_i, \lambda)$
    \State $\mathbf{x}_i \leftarrow \text{QPU.sample}(\text{BQM}_i)$
    \State Merge with conflict resolution: $\mathbf{x} \leftarrow \text{merge}(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x}_i)$
\EndFor
\State \Return $\mathbf{x}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\paragraph{Constraint Extraction}

The sub-CQM extraction process preserves constraints:

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Extract Sub-CQM}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require CQM, partition variables $\mathcal{P}$, fixed variables $\mathcal{F}_{\text{vars}}$
\Ensure Sub-CQM containing only $\mathcal{P}$ variables
\State Create new CQM: $\text{Sub-CQM} \leftarrow \emptyset$
\For{constraint $c \in \text{CQM.constraints}$}
    \State $\mathcal{V}_c \leftarrow$ variables in constraint $c$
    \State $\mathcal{V}_{\text{partition}} \leftarrow \mathcal{V}_c \cap \mathcal{P}$
    \State $\mathcal{V}_{\text{fixed}} \leftarrow \mathcal{V}_c \cap \mathcal{F}_{\text{vars}}$
    \If{$\mathcal{V}_{\text{partition}} \neq \emptyset$}
        \State Substitute fixed values into constraint
        \State Add simplified constraint to Sub-CQM
    \EndIf
\EndFor
\State \Return Sub-CQM
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\paragraph{Advantages}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Constraint preservation}: Constraints remain explicit within partitions
\item \textbf{Better penalty encoding}: Lagrange multipliers applied per partition
\item \textbf{Two-phase coordination}: Master-subproblem structure ensures global feasibility
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Method 6: Coordinated Master-Subproblem}

\paragraph{Description}
Coordinated decomposition uses a rigorous two-level optimization where the master problem selects which crops to use (U variables) and plot subproblems independently assign these crops to plots. This hierarchical structure is inspired by classical decomposition techniques such as Benders decomposition~\cite{geoffrion1972generalized} and the master-subproblem framework for large-scale optimization~\cite{lasdon1970optimization}.

\paragraph{Mathematical Formulation}

\textbf{Master Problem:}
\begin{align}
\text{minimize} \quad & \sum_{c \in \mathcal{C}} \lambda_c \cdot U_c \label{eq:master_obj}\\
\text{subject to} \quad & \sum_{c \in G_g} U_c \geq m_g, \quad \forall g \in \mathcal{G} \label{eq:master_fg}\\
& U_c \in \{0,1\}, \quad \forall c \in \mathcal{C}
\end{align}

The master objective uses small penalties $\lambda_c$ to encourage crop selection while satisfying food group diversity.

\textbf{Plot Subproblems (for each plot $f$):}
\begin{align}
\text{maximize} \quad & \sum_{c \in \mathcal{C}} b_c \cdot L_p \cdot Y_{f,c} \label{eq:sub_obj}\\
\text{subject to} \quad & \sum_{c \in \mathcal{C}} Y_{f,c} \leq 1 \label{eq:sub_one_crop}\\
& Y_{f,c} \leq U_c^*, \quad \forall c \in \mathcal{C} \label{eq:sub_u_fixed}\\
& Y_{f,c} \in \{0,1\}, \quad \forall c \in \mathcal{C}
\end{align}

where $U_c^*$ is the fixed value from the master solution.

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Coordinated Master-Subproblem Decomposition}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Data with plots $\mathcal{F}$, crops $\mathcal{C}$, food groups $\mathcal{G}$
\Ensure Solution $\mathbf{x} = (\mathbf{Y}, \mathbf{U})$
\State \textbf{Step 1: Solve Master Problem}
\State Build master BQM for U variables with food group constraints
\State $\mathbf{U}^* \leftarrow \text{QPU.sample}(\text{BQM}_{\text{master}})$
\State $\text{selected\_crops} \leftarrow \{c : U_c^* = 1\}$
\State \textbf{Step 2: Solve Plot Subproblems}
\For{each plot $f \in \mathcal{F}$}
    \State Build plot BQM with objective $\max \sum_{c \in \text{selected\_crops}} b_c \cdot L_p \cdot Y_{f,c}$
    \State Add one-crop constraint: $\sum_{c} Y_{f,c} \leq 1$
    \State Add U-linking: $Y_{f,c} \leq U_c^*$ encoded as penalty
    \State $\mathbf{Y}_f^* \leftarrow \text{QPU.sample}(\text{BQM}_f)$
    \State $\mathbf{Y}[f, :] \leftarrow \mathbf{Y}_f^*$
\EndFor
\State \Return $\mathbf{x} = (\mathbf{Y}, \mathbf{U}^*)$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\paragraph{Properties}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Hierarchical}: Clear master-subproblem structure
\item \textbf{Independent subproblems}: Plots solved in parallel
\item \textbf{Global constraint enforcement}: Master ensures food group diversity
\item \textbf{QPU calls}: $1 + |\mathcal{F}|$ (one master + one per plot)
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Method 7: Spectral Clustering}

\paragraph{Description}
Spectral clustering uses the eigenvectors of the graph Laplacian to partition variables, grouping tightly connected components while cutting weak connections. This method leverages the spectral properties of graphs~\cite{vonluxburg2007tutorial, ng2001spectral} and uses normalized cuts~\cite{shi2000normalized} to achieve balanced partitions suitable for quantum hardware constraints.

\paragraph{Mathematical Formulation}

Given interaction graph $G = (\mathcal{V}, E)$, compute:

\textbf{Adjacency matrix:} $A_{ij} = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } (i,j) \in E \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$

\textbf{Degree matrix:} $D_{ii} = \sum_j A_{ij}$

\textbf{Normalized Laplacian:} $\mathcal{L} = I -- D^{-1/2} A D^{-1/2}$

\textbf{Spectral embedding:} Compute eigenvectors $\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k$ corresponding to smallest eigenvalues

\textbf{Clustering:} Apply k-means on the embedding matrix $V = [\mathbf{v}_1 | \cdots | \mathbf{v}_k]$

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Spectral Clustering Decomposition}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Interaction graph $G = (\mathcal{V}, E)$, number of clusters $k$
\Ensure Partition set $\mathcal{P} = \{\mathcal{P}_1, \ldots, \mathcal{P}_k\}$
\State Construct adjacency matrix $A$ from $G$
\State Compute degree matrix $D$
\State Compute normalized Laplacian: $\mathcal{L} = I -- D^{-1/2} A D^{-1/2}$
\State Compute $k$ smallest eigenvectors: $V = [\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k]$
\State Apply k-means clustering on rows of $V$ to get cluster assignments
\For{cluster $i = 1$ to $k$}
    \State $\mathcal{P}_i \leftarrow$ variables assigned to cluster $i$
\EndFor
\State \Return $\mathcal{P}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\paragraph{Characteristics}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Spectral properties}: Uses graph spectrum for optimal cuts
\item \textbf{Balanced partitions}: k-means encourages similar partition sizes
\item \textbf{Computationally expensive}: Eigenvalue decomposition $O(|\mathcal{V}|^3)$
\item \textbf{Fixed partition count}: User specifies $k$
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Method 8: HybridGrid Decomposition}

\paragraph{Description}
HybridGrid partitioning creates a 2D grid structure by dividing both plots \emph{and} crops simultaneously. This produces many small partitions that are easy to embed while maintaining local constraint coherence. The approach is inspired by domain decomposition methods used in parallel computing and numerical PDEs~\cite{toselli2005domain, smith1996domain}, adapted specifically for the bipartite structure of plot-crop assignment problems.

\paragraph{Mathematical Formulation}

Given group sizes $k_F$ for plots and $k_C$ for crops, create a grid of partitions:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{P}_{\text{HybridGrid}} = \{\mathcal{P}_{(i,j)} : i \in [1, \lceil |\mathcal{F}|/k_F \rceil], j \in [1, \lceil |\mathcal{C}|/k_C \rceil]\} \cup \{\mathcal{P}_U\}
\end{equation}

where each grid cell contains:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{P}_{(i,j)} = \{Y_{f,c} : f \in \mathcal{F}_{[k_F(i-1)+1:k_F \cdot i]}, c \in \mathcal{C}_{[k_C(j-1)+1:k_C \cdot j]}\}
\end{equation}

For example, with $k_F = 5$ plots and $k_C = 9$ crops:
\begin{itemize}
\item Partition size: $5 \times 9 = 45$ variables (very easy to embed)
\item For 100 plots: $20 \times 3 = 60$ grid partitions + 1 U partition
\item For 1000 plots: $200 \times 3 = 600$ grid partitions + 1 U partition
\end{itemize}

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{HybridGrid Decomposition}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Plot group size $k_F$, crop group size $k_C$
\Ensure Partition set $\mathcal{P}$
\State $\mathcal{P} \leftarrow \emptyset$
\For{$i = 0$ to $\lfloor |\mathcal{F}|/k_F \rfloor$}
    \State $\mathcal{F}_i \leftarrow \{f_{k_F \cdot i + 1}, \ldots, f_{\min(k_F(i+1), |\mathcal{F}|)}\}$
    \For{$j = 0$ to $\lfloor |\mathcal{C}|/k_C \rfloor$}
        \State $\mathcal{C}_j \leftarrow \{c_{k_C \cdot j + 1}, \ldots, c_{\min(k_C(j+1), |\mathcal{C}|)}\}$
        \State $\mathcal{P} \leftarrow \mathcal{P} \cup \{\{Y_{f,c} : f \in \mathcal{F}_i, c \in \mathcal{C}_j\}\}$
    \EndFor
\EndFor
\State $\mathcal{P}_U \leftarrow \{U_c : c \in \mathcal{C}\}$
\State \Return $\mathcal{P} \cup \{\mathcal{P}_U\}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\paragraph{Key Advantages}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Small partition size}: $k_F \times k_C$ variables (typically 27-65) ensures easy embedding
\item \textbf{No embedding failures}: Partitions fit easily on QPU Pegasus topology
\item \textbf{Consistent performance}: Predictable partition sizes across all problem scales
\item \textbf{Constraint locality}: Each partition covers a coherent subset of the problem
\item \textbf{Linear scaling}: Number of partitions scales as $O(|\mathcal{F}| / k_F)$
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Note on Quantum Annealing Decomposition}
The challenge of embedding large optimization problems onto quantum annealers with limited qubit connectivity has motivated extensive research into partitioning strategies~\cite{booth2017partitioning, bian2020solving, date2019efficiently}. The methods presented here represent adaptations of classical decomposition techniques to the unique constraints and opportunities of quantum hardware.




\subsection{Mapping to D-Wave QPU: Decomposition Strategies for Study 2.B}

Direct embedding of large-scale optimization problems on current quantum annealing hardware faces significant challenges. The D-Wave Advantage system features over 5,600 qubits arranged in a Pegasus topology~\cite{dwave2020advantage, dwave2022pegasus}, where each qubit connects to approximately 15 neighbors. This represents a substantial improvement over the previous Chimera topology (6 connections per qubit)~\cite{boothler2022benchmarking}, but remains far from the all-to-all connectivity required by many optimization problems. Problems requiring dense connectivity between logical variables must use chains of physical qubits, which consumes the qubit budget rapidly and introduces chain break errors~\cite{date2019efficiently}. For our 100-plot instances with 1,800 variables, direct embedding is infeasible given current hardware constraints.

To overcome this limitation, we developed hierarchical decomposition strategies that partition the global problem into subproblems small enough to embed efficiently on the QPU while maintaining solution quality through iterative coordination. Our approach is motivated by recent advances in quantum annealing decomposition~\cite{booth2017partitioning, bass2021optimizing}, which demonstrate that problem-specific partitioning strategies can significantly improve solution quality compared to generic decomposition methods. The challenge of embedding large optimization problems onto quantum annealers with limited qubit connectivity has motivated extensive research into partitioning strategies~\cite{bian2020solving, yarkoni2022quantum, okada2020breaking}. Our key insight is that subproblems of 18 or fewer variables can be embedded as native cliques on the Pegasus topology, requiring no chains and thus eliminating embedding overhead entirely~\cite{dwave2022pegasus}.

\subsubsection{Strategy 1: Clique Decomposition (Plot-by-Plot)}

The clique decomposition strategy treats each plot as an independent subproblem, solving the crop allocation for that plot across all three time periods. This domain-aware partitioning exploits the natural independence structure of the agricultural planning problem, similar to decomposition strategies used in quantum annealing for scheduling~\cite{venturelli2015quantum} and graph coloring problems~\cite{titiloye2011quantum}. Each subproblem has $C \times T = 6 \times 3 = 18$ binary variables, which fits perfectly within a native clique on the Pegasus topology.

\paragraph{Mathematical Formulation}

For each plot $f \in \mathcal{F}$, we construct a plot-specific optimization problem over variables $\{Y_{p,c,t}\}_{c \in \mathcal{C}, t \in \mathcal{T}}$ where $\mathcal{C}$ represents the 6 crop families and $\mathcal{T} = \{1,2,3\}$ represents the three time periods. The plot subproblem objective incorporates three components:

\begin{align}
Z_f = & \sum_{c \in \mathcal{C}} \sum_{t \in \mathcal{T}} \frac{B_c \cdot A_f}{A_{\text{total}}} \cdot Y_{p,c,t} \label{eq:plot_benefit} \\
& + \sum_{t=2}^{T} \sum_{c, c' \in \mathcal{C}} \frac{\gamma_{\text{rot}} \cdot R_{c, c'} \cdot A_f}{A_{\text{total}}} \cdot Y_{p,c,t-1} \cdot Y_{p,c',t} \label{eq:plot_rotation} \\
& + \sum_{f' \in \mathcal{N}(f)} \sum_{t \in \mathcal{T}} \sum_{c, c' \in \mathcal{C}} \frac{\gamma_{\text{spat}} \cdot S_{c,c'}}{A_{\text{total}}} \cdot Y_{f,c,t} \cdot Y_{f',c',t}^{(k-1)} \label{eq:plot_spatial}
\end{align}

where $Y_{f',c',t}^{(k-1)}$ denotes the assignment for neighboring plot $f'$ from the previous iteration $k-1$. The Binary Quadratic Model (BQM) is constructed by converting the objective to minimization form and adding penalty terms for the one-crop-per-period constraint~\cite{lucas2014ising}:

\begin{equation}
E_f(Y) = -Z_f + \lambda_{\text{penalty}} \sum_{t \in \mathcal{T}} \left(\sum_{c \in \mathcal{C}} Y_{p,c,t} -1\right)^2
\end{equation}

For each plot $f$, we construct a Binary Quadratic Model (BQM) over the variables $\{Y_{p,c,t}\}_{c \in \mathcal{C}, t \in \mathcal{T}}$. The linear terms encode the crop benefits (negated for minimization), the quadratic terms encode the rotation synergies between consecutive periods, and additional quadratic terms implement the one-hot penalty for each period~\cite{lucas2014ising}. The BQM is then submitted to the DWaveCliqueSampler, which automatically finds a native clique embedding and returns 100 samples per subproblem.

\paragraph{Iterative Boundary Refinement}

Because the spatial interaction terms couple different plots, a single pass through the decomposition ignores neighbor effects. To address this, we employ iterative refinement over 3 boundary iterations, a technique inspired by domain decomposition methods in parallel computing~\cite{toselli2005domain}. In each iteration after the first, we add small bias terms to each plot's BQM based on the solutions found for neighboring plots in the previous iteration. 

The bias terms are computed as:
\begin{equation}
b_{f,c,t}^{(k)} = \sum_{f' \in \mathcal{N}(f)} \sum_{c' \in \mathcal{C}} \gamma_{\text{spat}} \cdot S_{c,c'} \cdot Y_{f',c',t}^{(k-1)}
\end{equation}

These biases approximate the spatial coupling terms, encouraging compatible crop choices across plot boundaries. Empirically, 3 iterations are sufficient for convergence, reducing the optimality gap from 20-25\% (single iteration) to 11-15\% (three iterations).

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Clique Decomposition with Iterative Refinement}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Plots $\mathcal{F}$, crops $\mathcal{C}$, periods $\mathcal{T} = \{1,2,3\}$
\Require Neighbor graph $\mathcal{N}(f)$ for each plot $f$
\Ensure Solution $\mathbf{Y} = \{Y_{p,c,t}\}$
\State Initialize: $Y_{p,c,t}^{(0)} \leftarrow 0$ for all $f, c, t$
\For{iteration $k = 1$ to $K_{\text{max}}$ (typically 3)}
    \For{each plot $f \in \mathcal{F}$}
        \State Compute neighbor biases: $b_{f,c,t}^{(k)} \leftarrow \sum_{f' \in \mathcal{N}(f)} \sum_{c'} \gamma_{\text{spat}} \cdot S_{c,c'} \cdot Y_{f',c',t}^{(k-1)}$
        \State Build BQM: $E_f = -Z_f + \lambda \sum_t (\sum_c Y_{p,c,t} -1)^2 -- \sum_{c,t} b_{f,c,t}^{(k)} \cdot Y_{p,c,t}$
        \State Embed BQM on QPU: $\phi_f \leftarrow \text{DWaveCliqueSampler.find\_embedding}(E_f)$
        \State Sample: $\mathbf{Y}_f^{(k)} \leftarrow \text{QPU.sample}(E_f, \phi_f, n_{\text{reads}} = 100)$
        \State Update: $Y_{p,c,t}^{(k)} \leftarrow \mathbf{Y}_f^{(k)}[c,t]$ (best sample)
    \EndFor
    \State Evaluate global objective $Z_{\text{global}}(\mathbf{Y}^{(k)})$
    \If{convergence criterion met}
        \State \textbf{break}
    \EndIf
\EndFor
\State \Return $\mathbf{Y}^{(K_{\text{max}})}$ (best across all iterations)
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The algorithm proceeds as follows: we initialize all solutions to an empty assignment, then for each iteration from 1 to 3, we loop over all plots, building the BQM with neighbor biases from the previous iteration, sampling from the QPU, and updating the solution for that plot. After each iteration, we evaluate the global objective and retain the best solution found. This approach is highly parallelizable; in principle, all plot subproblems within an iteration could be solved simultaneously on separate QPU calls, similar to parallel quantum annealing strategies~\cite{pelofske2021parallel}.

\paragraph{Complexity Analysis}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Subproblem size}: 18 variables per plot (fits native Pegasus cliques)
\item \textbf{QPU calls}: $K_{\text{max}} \times |\mathcal{F}|$ (typically $3 \times F$)
\item \textbf{Embedding time}: Near-zero (native clique, no chains)
\item \textbf{Chain breaks}: $<$1\% (minimal chaining required)
\item \textbf{Convergence}: 3 iterations sufficient empirically
\item \textbf{Parallelization potential}: $O(1)$ time with $|\mathcal{F}|$ QPUs
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Strategy 2: Spatial-Temporal Decomposition}

The spatial-temporal decomposition strategy takes a different approach, partitioning the problem along both spatial and temporal dimensions simultaneously. This multi-dimensional decomposition is motivated by the observation that both spatial clustering and temporal decoupling can reduce problem complexity while preserving essential structure~\cite{ushijima2017graph}. Plots are grouped into spatial clusters of 2 to 5 neighboring plots using a simple nearest-neighbor heuristic, preserving local spatial interactions within each cluster. Within each cluster, we further slice by time period, solving one period at a time.

\paragraph{Mathematical Formulation}

Given a spatial clustering $\mathcal{F} = \bigcup_{i=1}^{N_c} \mathcal{F}_i$ where each cluster $\mathcal{F}_i$ contains $k_i \leq 5$ neighboring plots, we create cluster-period subproblems:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{P}_{i,t} = \{Y_{p,c,t} : f \in \mathcal{F}_i, c \in \mathcal{C}\}
\end{equation}

Each subproblem optimizes crop allocation for cluster $i$ in period $t$, with constraints:
\begin{align}
\text{maximize} \quad & \sum_{f \in \mathcal{F}_i} \sum_{c \in \mathcal{C}} \frac{B_c \cdot A_f}{A_{\text{total}}} \cdot Y_{p,c,t} \\
& + \sum_{(p_1,p_2) \in \mathcal{E}_i} \sum_{c,c' \in \mathcal{C}} \frac{\gamma_{\text{spat}} \cdot S_{c,c'}}{A_{\text{total}}} \cdot Y_{p_1,c,t} \cdot Y_{p_2,c',t} \label{eq:intra_cluster_spatial}
\end{align}

where $\mathcal{E}_i$ is the set of edges within cluster $i$. For period $t > 1$, rotation effects are incorporated by fixing previous period assignments:

\begin{equation}
\text{Rotation term:} \quad \sum_{f \in \mathcal{F}_i} \sum_{c,c' \in \mathcal{C}} \frac{\gamma_{\text{rot}} \cdot R_{c,c'} \cdot A_f}{A_{\text{total}}} \cdot Y_{p,c,t-1}^* \cdot Y_{p,c',t}
\end{equation}

where $Y_{p,c,t-1}^*$ are fixed assignments from the previous temporal slice.

This results in subproblems with at most $3 \text{ plots} \times 6 \text{ crops} \times 1 \text{ period} = 18$ variables, again fitting within native cliques. The temporal slicing allows rotation synergies within clusters to be handled through sequential solving: when solving period $t$, the solutions from period $t-1$ are fixed, providing a boundary condition that captures the rotation effects. This sequential approach is analogous to time-stepping methods in numerical simulation, where future states are computed based on fixed past states.

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Spatial-Temporal Decomposition}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Plots $\mathcal{F}$, spatial clustering $\{\mathcal{F}_1, \ldots, \mathcal{F}_{N_c}\}$
\Require Crops $\mathcal{C}$, periods $\mathcal{T} = \{1,2,3\}$
\Ensure Solution $\mathbf{Y}$
\State Initialize: $\mathbf{Y} \leftarrow \emptyset$
\For{period $t = 1$ to $T$}
    \For{each cluster $\mathcal{F}_i$}
        \State Build cluster-period BQM for variables $\{Y_{p,c,t} : f \in \mathcal{F}_i, c \in \mathcal{C}\}$
        \State Include intra-cluster spatial terms (Equation~\ref{eq:intra_cluster_spatial})
        \If{$t > 1$}
            \State Add rotation terms with fixed $Y_{f,c,t-1}^*$ from previous period
        \EndIf
        \State Add inter-cluster boundary biases from neighboring clusters
        \State Embed and solve on QPU: $\mathbf{Y}_i^{(t)} \leftarrow \text{QPU.sample}(\text{BQM}_{i,t})$
        \State Update: $\mathbf{Y}[f,c,t] \leftarrow \mathbf{Y}_i^{(t)}[f,c]$ for all $f \in \mathcal{F}_i$
    \EndFor
    \State Perform boundary refinement iteration across clusters
\EndFor
\State \Return $\mathbf{Y}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

Boundary coordination is required both between spatial clusters (to handle inter-cluster spatial interactions) and between time periods (already handled by sequential solving). The iterative refinement process is similar to clique decomposition but operates over cluster-period pairs rather than individual plots.

This strategy is particularly effective when spatial interactions are strong, as it explicitly preserves neighbor relationships within clusters rather than approximating them through bias terms alone.

\paragraph{Clustering Strategy}

Spatial clusters are formed using a greedy nearest-neighbor algorithm:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Sort plots by spatial coordinates
    \item Initialize first cluster with first plot
    \item For each remaining plot $f$:
        \begin{itemize}
            \item Find cluster $\mathcal{F}_i$ with nearest centroid
            \item If $|\mathcal{F}_i| < k_{\max}$ (typically 5), add $f$ to $\mathcal{F}_i$
            \item Otherwise, create new cluster with $f$
        \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

This produces spatially coherent clusters with bounded size, ensuring subproblems remain embeddable.

\paragraph{Complexity Analysis}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Subproblem size}: $\leq 18$ variables per cluster-period
\item \textbf{Number of clusters}: $\lceil |\mathcal{F}| / k_{\max} \rceil$ (typically $\lceil F/5 \rceil$)
\item \textbf{QPU calls}: $T \times N_c = 3 \times \lceil F/5 \rceil$
\item \textbf{Advantage}: Preserves spatial structure within clusters
\item \textbf{Disadvantage}: Requires sequential temporal solving (no time parallelization)
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Strategy 3: Hierarchical Multi-Level Approach}

For the largest problem instances (50 to 100 plots), we employ a three-level hierarchical approach that combines aggregation with spatial decomposition. This method draws inspiration from multilevel graph partitioning techniques~\cite{karypis1998fast, hendrickson1995multilevel} and recent work on hierarchical quantum optimization~\cite{dash2025hierarchicalquantumoptimizationlargescale}. At the first level, crops are aggregated from 27 foods to 6 families, reducing the variable count by 4.5$\times$. At the second level, plots are partitioned into spatial clusters of approximately 5 plots each, based on k-means clustering of plot coordinates. At the third level, each cluster is solved on the QPU as a single subproblem with $5 \times 6 \times 3 = 90$ variables.

\paragraph{Three-Level Hierarchy}

\textbf{Level 1 (Crop Aggregation):} Map 27 individual crops to 6 crop families:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{C}_{\text{fine}} \rightarrow \mathcal{C}_{\text{coarse}}, \quad |\mathcal{C}_{\text{coarse}}| = 6
\end{equation}

Aggregation preserves food group structure and nutritional characteristics through weighted averaging of benefit scores.

\textbf{Level 2 (Spatial Clustering):} Partition plots into $N_c = \lceil |\mathcal{F}| / 5 \rceil$ clusters using k-means on $(x_f, y_f)$ coordinates:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{F} = \bigcup_{i=1}^{N_c} \mathcal{F}_i, \quad |\mathcal{F}_i| \approx 5
\end{equation}

\textbf{Level 3 (Cluster Optimization):} Each cluster subproblem contains:
\begin{equation}
|\mathcal{P}_i| = |\mathcal{F}_i| \times |\mathcal{C}_{\text{coarse}}| \times T \approx 5 \times 6 \times 3 = 90 \text{ variables}
\end{equation}

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Hierarchical Multi-Level Decomposition}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Plots $\mathcal{F}$, fine crops $\mathcal{C}_{\text{fine}}$ (27), periods $\mathcal{T}$
\Ensure Solution $\mathbf{Y}$
\State \textbf{Level 1: Crop Aggregation}
\State $\mathcal{C}_{\text{coarse}} \leftarrow \text{aggregate\_crops}(\mathcal{C}_{\text{fine}})$ \Comment{27 $\to$ 6 families}
\State Compute aggregated benefits: $B_g \leftarrow \text{weighted\_avg}(\{B_c : c \in \text{family } g\})$
\State \textbf{Level 2: Spatial Clustering}
\State $\{\mathcal{F}_1, \ldots, \mathcal{F}_{N_c}\} \leftarrow \text{k\_means\_cluster}(\mathcal{F}, k=5)$
\State \textbf{Level 3: Cluster Solving with Coordination}
\For{iteration $k = 1$ to $K_{\text{coord}}$ (typically 3)}
    \For{each cluster $i = 1$ to $N_c$}
        \State Build cluster BQM with 90 variables: $\{Y_{p,c,t} : f \in \mathcal{F}_i, c \in \mathcal{C}_{\text{coarse}}, t \in \mathcal{T}\}$
        \State Add temporal rotation terms (within cluster)
        \State Add spatial terms (intra-cluster and boundary biases)
        \State Embed BQM: $\phi_i \leftarrow \text{MinorMiner}(\text{BQM}_i, G_{\text{Pegasus}})$
        \State Sample: $\mathbf{Y}_i^{(k)} \leftarrow \text{QPU.sample}(\text{BQM}_i, \phi_i, n_{\text{reads}}=100)$
        \State Update global solution: $\mathbf{Y}^{(k)} \leftarrow \text{merge}(\mathbf{Y}^{(k)}, \mathbf{Y}_i^{(k)})$
    \EndFor
\EndFor
\State \textbf{Optional: Disaggregation}
\State $\mathbf{Y}_{\text{fine}} \leftarrow \text{disaggregate}(\mathbf{Y}, \mathcal{C}_{\text{coarse}} \rightarrow \mathcal{C}_{\text{fine}})$
\State \Return $\mathbf{Y}$ or $\mathbf{Y}_{\text{fine}}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

Subproblems of 90 variables require embedding rather than native clique solving, introducing some overhead. However, the DWaveCliqueSampler can still find efficient embeddings for problems of this size on the Pegasus topology~\cite{boothler2022benchmarking}, and the reduced number of subproblems (20 clusters for 100 plots) reduces the total number of QPU calls. The improved connectivity of Pegasus compared to Chimera topology results in shorter chain lengths, which correlates with better solution quality and faster convergence~\cite{dwave2020advantage}. Boundary coordination between clusters follows the same iterative refinement approach, with 3 iterations typically sufficient for convergence.

\paragraph{Embedding Considerations}

Unlike the previous strategies that fit within native cliques, 90-variable subproblems require chain-based embedding:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Expected chain length}: 2-4 physical qubits per logical variable
    \item \textbf{Embedding time}: 5-30 seconds per cluster (varies with connectivity)
    \item \textbf{Chain break rate}: $<$3\% with auto-scaled chain strength
    \item \textbf{Mitigation}: Post-processing with majority vote on chain qubits
\end{itemize}

This hierarchical approach scales well to problem sizes beyond our current test range, as the cluster size and aggregation level can be adjusted to balance subproblem size against coordination overhead. The key trade-off is between the number of QPU calls (which increases linearly with the number of clusters) and the embedding quality (which degrades with larger subproblems). For problems requiring more than 100 variables per subproblem, hybrid quantum-classical decomposition methods offer a promising path forward, combining quantum annealing for subproblems with classical optimization for coordination.

\paragraph{Scalability Analysis}

The hierarchical method achieves favorable scaling:
\begin{equation}
\text{QPU calls} = K_{\text{coord}} \times \lceil |\mathcal{F}| / 5 \rceil \approx 3 \times F/5 = 0.6F
\end{equation}

compared to clique decomposition's $3F$ calls, achieving a $\sim$5$\times$ reduction in QPU access overhead. The trade-off is larger subproblems requiring non-trivial embedding.

\subsection{Grid Refinement Analysis}\label{app:refinement}

\subsubsection{Motivation and Research Question}

The binary (PATCH) formulation discretizes continuous land into fixed-size plots with binary assignment variables. This introduces an \textbf{approximation error} compared to the continuous formulation. The fundamental question is:

\begin{center}
\textit{How does grid refinement (number of plots) affect solution quality?}
\end{center}

\textbf{Hypothesis:} Finer grids ($n \to \infty$) should converge to the continuous optimum, but at the cost of increased problem size and solve time.

\subsubsection{Experimental Design}

\textbf{Grid Refinement Levels Tested:}
$$n \in \{5, 10, 25, 50, 100\}$$

\textbf{Comparison Framework:}

For each refinement level $n$:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Continuous Baseline:} Solve with $n$ plots using \textbf{uneven distribution} (realistic sizes from \texttt{plot\_sampler})
    \item \textbf{Discretized:} Solve with $n$ plots using \textbf{even grid} (equal-sized plots via \texttt{plot\_sampler})
\end{enumerate}

Both scenarios use the same total land area for fair comparison.

\textbf{Evaluation Metrics:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Objective Value:} $Z_{\text{cont}}$ (continuous) vs. $Z_{\text{disc}}$ (discretized)
    \item \textbf{Optimality Gap:} $\Delta = \frac{Z_{\text{cont}} -- Z_{\text{disc}}}{Z_{\text{cont}}} \times 100\%$
    \item \textbf{Solve Time:} Wall-clock time for PuLP/Gurobi
    \item \textbf{Time Ratio:} $t_{\text{disc}} / t_{\text{cont}}$
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Implementation Details}

The test script (\texttt{Grid\_Refinement.py}) executes the following workflow:

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Grid Refinement Convergence Study}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State \textbf{Input:} Total land area $A_{\text{total}}$
\For{$n \in \{5, 10, 25, 50, 100, 200\}$}
    \State \textbf{// Continuous Baseline}
    \State Generate $n$ plots with uneven distribution (total area $A_{\text{total}}$)
    \State Load food data (2 foods per group for tractability)
    \State Create continuous CQM with $A_{f,c}$ and $Y_{f,c}$ variables
    \State Solve with PuLP/Gurobi $\to$ $Z_{\text{cont}}, t_{\text{cont}}$
    \State
    \State \textbf{// Discretized Formulation}
    \State Generate $n$ plots with even grid (total area $A_{\text{total}}$, equal plot size $a_p = A_{\text{total}}/n$)
    \State Load same food data
    \State Create binary CQM with $Y_{p,c}$ variables only
    \State Solve with PuLP/Gurobi $\to$ $Z_{\text{disc}}, t_{\text{disc}}$
    \State
    \State Compute gap: $\Delta = (Z_{\text{cont}} -- Z_{\text{disc}})/Z_{\text{cont}} \times 100\%$
    \State Record: $n, Z_{\text{cont}}, Z_{\text{disc}}, \Delta, t_{\text{cont}}, t_{\text{disc}}, t_{\text{disc}}/t_{\text{cont}}$
\EndFor
\State \textbf{Output:} Table and convergence analysis
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsubsection{Theoretical Analysis}

\paragraph{Approximation Error Bound:}

For a linear objective $f(A) = \sum_c B_c A_c$, the discretization error is:

$$\epsilon(n) = \left| \sum_c B_c A_c^{\text{opt}} -- \sum_c B_c \left(\sum_p a_p Y_{p,c}^{\text{opt}}\right) \right|$$

where $A_c^{\text{opt}}$ is the continuous optimal area and $Y_{p,c}^{\text{opt}}$ is the discrete solution.

\textbf{Upper Bound:} If minimum planting areas $A_{\min,c}$ dominate, the error is bounded by:
$$\epsilon(n) \leq \sum_c B_c \cdot a_p = O\left(\frac{A_{\text{total}}}{n}\right)$$

\textbf{Convergence Rate:} $\epsilon(n) = O(n^{-1})$.

\paragraph{Computational Complexity Trade-off:}\\

\textbf{Continuous Formulation:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Variables: $2nc$ ($n$ plots, $c$ crops)
    \item Constraints: $O(nc)$
    \item Solve time: $O(2^{nc} \cdot \text{poly}(nc))$ (MILP worst-case)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Binary Formulation:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Variables: $nc$ (binary only)
    \item Constraints: $O(n + c)$ (fewer due to simpler structure)
    \item Solve time: $O(2^{nc} \cdot \text{poly}(nc))$ (BIP worst-case, but better LP relaxation)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Expected Behavior:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Small $n$ ($n \leq 25$): Binary faster (fewer variables, simpler constraints)
    \item Large $n$ ($n \geq 100$): Continuous may be faster (continuous relaxation tighter than binary LP relaxation)
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Cluster-Based Decomposition Structure}
\label{sub:clusterbased}

The hierarchical method partitions the problem by \emph{plots}, not by variables. Given $n_{\text{plots}}$ plots, approximately $n_{\text{plots}}/9$ clusters are created:
$$n_{\text{clusters}} \approx \left\lceil \frac{n_{\text{plots}}}{9} \right\rceil$$
Each cluster is solved independently on the QPU, incurring classical overhead for embedding ($\sim$6--8\,s per cluster) and coordination between cluster boundaries. The total number of variables $N = n_{\text{plots}} \times n_{\text{foods}} \times 3$ grows with both plot count and food granularity, but the \emph{decomposition overhead scales only with plot count}.

\paragraph{Implications for 6-Food vs 27-Food (Agg.) Comparison}

This plot-based decomposition creates a critical difference when comparing formulations at the same variable count. The number of plots required to reach $N$ variables is:
\begin{align}
    \text{6-Food:} \quad n_{\text{plots}} &= \frac{N}{6 \times 3} = \frac{N}{18} \\
    \text{27-Food (Agg.):} \quad n_{\text{plots}} &= \frac{N}{27 \times 3} = \frac{N}{81}
\end{align}

\noindent The ratio is $81/18 = 4.5$, meaning 6-Food requires \textbf{4.5$\times$ more plots} than 27-Food (Agg.) to achieve the same variable count. Since cluster count scales with plots, this directly translates to 4.5$\times$ more clusters and thus $\sim$4$\times$ higher total solve time for 6-Food (Table~\ref{tab:formulation_comparison}).
